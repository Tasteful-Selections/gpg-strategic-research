# Competitive Analysis Framework

How to benchmark competitors, map landscapes, and analyze strategic moves in produce and adjacent markets.

## When This Applies

When Jon asks about competitors, competitive positioning, market share dynamics, "how do we compare to...", or wants a landscape view of a market segment. Also consult when building or updating `context/competitor-landscape.md`.

## Workflow

### Landscape Mapping

When mapping a competitive landscape (like the initial 20+ company map), organize by segment:

1. **Direct produce competitors** — companies competing for the same shelf space in the same categories (Bolthouse, Dole fresh veg, Taylor Farms, etc.)
2. **Adjacent produce companies** — in produce but different categories; potential expansion competitors or acquisition targets
3. **Value-added / branded produce** — companies that have moved beyond commodity into branded, prepared, or snacking products
4. **Emerging / specialty players** — smaller companies with innovative models or niche positions worth watching

For each company, capture the data points Jon specified: revenue/scale estimates, category overlap with GPG, key retail accounts (where known), and geographic footprint.

Use web research to populate. Start with the most direct competitors (Bolthouse, Dole, Taylor Farms, Earthbound/Taylor Organic, Green Giant/B&G Foods), then expand outward. Accept that data on private companies will be incomplete — note confidence level for each estimate.

### Company-Level Analysis

When Jon asks about a specific competitor, go deeper:

1. **What do they do?** Products, categories, channels, scale.
2. **Where do they overlap with GPG?** Same categories, same retailers, same geographies.
3. **What are they doing strategically?** Recent acquisitions, product launches, partnerships, leadership changes. Search news from the last 12 months.
4. **What's their advantage?** What do they have that GPG doesn't? Brand recognition, technology, distribution, specific retailer relationships?
5. **What's their vulnerability?** Where are they weak? Over-leveraged, losing share, operational problems, ownership instability?
6. **So what for GPG?** Is this a threat, an opportunity, or both? What should Jon be watching?

### Benchmarking

When comparing GPG to competitors on specific dimensions:

- State what's known vs. estimated. "GPG's carrot volume is [X, if Jon provides it]. Bolthouse is estimated at [Y, based on Z source]."
- Compare on dimensions that matter for the specific question, not a generic SWOT.
- Identify the one or two dimensions where the competitive gap matters most.

## Output Format

For landscape maps: use a structured table organized by segment. Include a confidence indicator for estimates (high/medium/low based on source quality).

For company deep-dives: narrative format with clear sections. Lead with the "so what for GPG" — don't bury it at the end.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Treating estimates as facts.** Revenue estimates for private produce companies are rough. Always qualify.
- **Generic SWOT instead of focused analysis.** Jon doesn't want a four-quadrant exercise. He wants "what does this mean for us on this specific question."
- **Stale data presented as current.** Produce industry moves fast. Check dates on everything. A 2022 article about Bolthouse may not reflect 2025 reality, especially post-ownership changes.
- **Missing the PE angle.** GPG is PE-backed. Competitors' ownership structures matter — a PE-backed competitor behaves differently than a family-owned one. Note ownership when relevant.
