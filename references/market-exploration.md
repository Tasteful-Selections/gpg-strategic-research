# Market Exploration Framework

How to evaluate new products, services, channels, and adjacent market opportunities for GPG.

## When This Applies

When Jon asks "should we get into...", "what if we...", "where's the whitespace", or explores any expansion idea — new products, new services, new channels, adjacent markets. Also when thinking about innovation within existing categories.

## Workflow

### Step 1: Define the Opportunity Space

Jon's exploration questions can range from specific ("should GPG do meal kits") to wide open ("where could we expand"). Start by defining the boundaries:

- **Category exploration**: New product types within or adjacent to current categories
- **Value-add exploration**: Moving up the value chain — processed, prepared, branded, snacking
- **Channel exploration**: Foodservice, DTC, e-commerce, international, institutional
- **Service model exploration**: Category management as a service, data/analytics offerings, co-packing
- **Adjacent market exploration**: New crop categories, adjacent food segments

If Jon's question spans multiple of these, break them apart and prioritize. Don't try to research everything at once.

### Step 2: Assess "Right to Win"

Before researching market attractiveness, check whether GPG has a credible basis to compete. Reference `context/gpg-capabilities.md` and evaluate:

- **Operational leverage**: Can GPG's farming, processing, or distribution infrastructure serve this opportunity without massive new investment?
- **Customer leverage**: Do GPG's existing retail relationships provide a path to shelf space or distribution for this product/service?
- **Category knowledge**: Does GPG's produce expertise translate, or is this a genuinely new domain?
- **Scale advantage**: Does GPG's size create cost, distribution, or negotiating advantages in this space?

Be honest. "GPG grows carrots really well" doesn't mean GPG can do meal kits. Name the gaps between current capabilities and what the opportunity requires.

### Step 3: Research Market Dynamics

For the opportunity space, research:

1. **Market size and growth** — how big is this, and which direction is it moving?
2. **Who's already there** — incumbent competitors, their scale, their positioning
3. **Consumer/buyer trends** — what's driving demand? Health, convenience, price, sustainability?
4. **Barriers to entry** — capital requirements, regulatory, technology, brand, distribution
5. **Recent moves** — who's entered or exited this space recently, and why?

### Step 4: Build or Buy Assessment

For any opportunity that passes the "right to win" filter:

- **Build**: What would it take for GPG to enter organically? Timeline, investment, risk.
- **Buy**: Are there acquisition targets that provide a faster path? (Hand off to M&A screening framework in `references/ma-screening.md`)
- **Partner**: Could GPG enter through a partnership or JV without full commitment?

### Step 5: Synthesize with Recommendation Framing

Don't just present research. Frame it as: "Here's what the opportunity looks like, here's where GPG has a right to win (and where it doesn't), and here are the paths to get there — ranked by risk-adjusted effort."

Always flag assumptions. "This assumes GPG's distribution network could handle [X] without significant new investment. If that's wrong, the economics change."

## Innovation Within Existing Categories

When the question is about innovating inside carrots, potatoes, or organic veg (not expanding to new markets):

1. Research what competitors are doing in the same category — new formats, packaging, positioning
2. Look at adjacent snack and fresh categories for ideas that could translate
3. Identify consumer trends driving category evolution
4. Evaluate against GPG's processing and distribution capabilities
5. Flag what's been tried before and failed (and why — the "why it failed" matters more than the fact that it did)

## Common Pitfalls

- **Falling in love with an idea.** Your job is to evaluate, not advocate. If the data says an opportunity is weak, say so even if it sounds exciting.
- **Ignoring the "right to win" step.** Market attractiveness without competitive advantage is a recipe for expensive failure.
- **Treating all expansion as equal.** A new carrot format leveraging existing infrastructure is fundamentally different from entering a new crop category. Scale the analysis to the actual risk.
- **Forgetting GPG's current integration complexity.** The company is consolidating 5 entities right now. Any expansion competes for management attention and operational capacity. Factor that in.
