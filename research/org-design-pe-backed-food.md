# Commercial Org Design at PE-Backed Multi-Entity Food Platforms

**Honest framing of the source set.** PE-backed private platforms are the most opaque category in food research. Public LinkedIn titles, isolated press releases on leadership appointments, S-1 filings (Sovos is the only true goldmine here), and lender/rating-agency commentary are most of what's on the table. Per-company specifics are partial. The cross-company *pattern* — what the PE playbook looks like — is more answerable than any individual case. This document marks confidence on every finding. Most cells are **inferred** from indirect signals; only the Sovos sales structure, the Wayne-Sanderson April 2026 reorg, and a handful of CEO appointments are **confirmed**.

---

## Sovos Brands (Advent International, 2017–2024)

The most documented case in this set, because Sovos filed an S-1 in 2021, traded publicly, and was acquired by Campbell in early 2024. Brands at peak: Rao's, Michael Angelo's, noosa, Birch Benders.

**1. Commercial scope.** Sovos ran a Chief Sales Officer (Risa Cretella) reporting to CEO Todd Lachman, with a separate Chief Commercial Officer title also appearing in some directories (Rich Greenberg). The split is material: a CSO owns the customer-facing sales force; a CCO can be a broader commercial-strategy seat (revenue management, category, shopper insights). Sovos appears to have run both, with CSO as the operating role. **Confirmed** (Bain interview, Sovos public filings).

**2. Entity/brand × function matrix.** This is the single most important finding in the set. Advent and Lachman explicitly built **one shared sales force** across Rao's, Michael Angelo's, noosa, and Birch Benders, while keeping **brand teams (marketing, brand management) dedicated**. Advent's own portfolio writeup describes "a unified and one of the best-in-class salesforces" — not "salesforces" plural. Bain's Cretella interview reinforces it: she frames sales as one team with cross-brand reach into retailers. **Confirmed.**

The brand side stayed separate. Risa Cretella later resurfaced inside Campbell as "EVP and Group GM, Dinner & Sauces" — meaning each brand's P&L was preserved as a GM-led unit even after the rollup. The model: **federated brands, integrated sales.** Brand teams own the strategy, innovation, marketing; sales force represents the whole portfolio at the buyer.

**3. Customer × category.** Sovos pitched retailers as a single counterparty. Cretella's quote — that Sovos could "command a seat at the table" alongside the giants because of insurgent growth — only works if the company shows up at Walmart, Kroger, Target as one team carrying four brands. **Inferred** but consistent with all public signal. The buyer relationship layer was almost certainly consolidated within months of each acquisition; brand marketing was not.

**4. The PE playbook.** Advent's pattern: install operating partner (Bill Johnson, ex-Heinz CEO) alongside founder-CEO (Lachman) at platform formation; build shared services and a shared sales force as the *first* scale lever; let brands stay distinct on marketing and innovation; bolt acquisitions onto the platform sequentially (Michael Angelo's 2017 → Rao's 2017 → noosa 2018 → Birch Benders 2020); divest the one that didn't fit (Birch Benders 2022); exit via IPO then strategic. **Confirmed.**

**5. Synergy capture.** Public framing emphasized **distribution expansion** (sell Rao's reach to noosa and Michael Angelo's, sell noosa's dairy-aisle access to Rao's adjacencies) and **shared back-office leverage** rather than aggressive cost takeout. The S-1 narrative was a growth story, not a cost story. **Confirmed via Advent and Bain commentary.**

**6. What broke.** Birch Benders divestiture in late 2022 is the visible failure — the brand didn't fit the premium-Italian-and-yogurt platform thesis and got cut. Confirms that even disciplined PE platforms make adjacency mistakes. **Confirmed.**

## Hearthside Food Solutions (Charlesbank, Partners Group, 2018–2024)

A different animal: contract manufacturer, B2B, no consumer brands. Customers are Mondelez, Kellogg, General Mills, PepsiCo, Kraft Heinz. The "brands × customers" tension that drives CPG rollups doesn't apply here.

**1. Commercial scope.** SVP/Chief Commercial Officer (Chuck Metzger), with SVP of Sales (Steve Russell) underneath. CEO transitions: Rich Scalise → Chuck Metzger period → Darlene Nicosia (2022, ex-Coca-Cola NA). VP of Sales Scott Goins runs a ~45-person sales team. **Confirmed** via press releases, RocketReach.

**2. Entity/brand × function matrix.** N/A in the brand sense. The relevant matrix is **plant × customer**: 38–43 facilities serving ~10–15 anchor customers under multi-year volume agreements (~65% of 2024 revenue). The commercial design tension here is whether each *customer* gets a dedicated team (key-account model) or whether plants get sold separately. Hearthside has run a **dedicated key-account model** — sales teams aligned to major brand customers, not to plants. **Inferred** from sales department size, customer concentration disclosures, and contract-manufacturer norms.

**3. Customer × category.** Hearthside's whole proposition is "act as an extension of the customer's brand team." That requires customer-aligned commercial teams. They're not pitching cross-sell across customers (Mondelez doesn't want to share knowledge with Kellogg). **Inferred** but high-confidence given the business model.

**4. The PE playbook here.** Charlesbank/Partners Group used Hearthside as an LBO-and-bolt-on platform: stack debt, acquire smaller co-mans, drive plant utilization. Commercial integration of acquired plants into the Hearthside customer book is the value lever. The playbook isn't "consolidate brand teams" — it's "absorb new plants into the existing key-account organization." Frequent CEO turnover (Scalise → others → Nicosia in 2022) is consistent with a PE pattern of rotating leadership when integration or financial targets slip.

**5. Synergy capture.** Lender presentations and Moody's commentary frame synergies as **plant utilization, customer cross-sell of capabilities (e.g., new packaging formats), and procurement leverage**. Not commercial-team consolidation per se. **Confirmed** via Moody's B3 → Caa1 commentary.

**6. What broke.** Moody's downgraded Hearthside to Caa1 by 2023, citing 9x debt/EBITDA and inability to restore positive cash flow. The 2023 press exposé of child-labor violations at supplier facilities created brand and customer-relationship damage. Both reflect a PE platform that overlevered, expanded too fast, and couldn't deliver promised synergies. **Confirmed.** Lesson: scale without commercial discipline destroys value.

## Shearer's Foods (Wind Point → Ontario Teachers → CD&R, 2007–present)

Private label and contract manufacturer of salty snacks. Multiple successive PE owners. Roll-up via Lance Private Brands (Snyder's-Lance) and Medallion Foods (ConAgra) acquisitions, doubling the business under Wind Point/Teachers' ownership.

**1. Commercial scope.** Wind Point installed CJ Fraleigh (ex-CEO Sara Lee North America) as Chairman/CEO at acquisition. The "top caliber CEO" pattern is explicit in Wind Point's own messaging — they describe partnering *with* a CEO rather than installing one after close. **Confirmed.**

**2. Entity/brand × function matrix.** Limited public signal on the function-by-function org. The Lance and Medallion acquisitions were **plant-and-capability adds** (Burlington IA, Ontario Canada plants; tortilla chips capability) more than brand acquisitions — Shearer's didn't acquire consumer brands so much as private-label books of business and manufacturing footprint. Commercial integration was therefore about **absorbing acquired customer contracts into Shearer's account team**, not consolidating brand teams. **Inferred.**

**3. Customer × category.** Private label means the retailer *is* the brand. Single account team per major retailer is the model. The complexity is making sure Shearer's serves Walmart's private-label kettle chip business with the same coordination across three plants. Branded business (Shearer's brand) is a smaller piece. **Inferred.**

**4. The PE playbook.** Wind Point's stated playbook: top CEO + add-on M&A + category-management capability build + productivity. Three years post-acquisition, Wind Point exited to Ontario Teachers (2015). Teachers held longer; CD&R bought it in 2023. The pattern: each PE owner runs a 3–7 year value-creation cycle, then sells to the next owner who runs a different lever. **Confirmed.**

**5. Synergy capture.** Productivity and category-management capability building, framed as customer-facing capability gains. Wind Point: "increasing Shearer's category management and manufacturing capabilities to help its customers grow." **Confirmed.**

**6. What broke.** No public failure stories. Successive sales suggest each owner made money. The model worked because the integration unit was *plant + customer contract*, not *brand team*. Lower org complexity than a CPG rollup.

## Wayne-Sanderson Farms (Continental Grain + Cargill JV, 2022–present)

Merger of Wayne Farms (Continental Grain subsidiary since 1965) with Sanderson Farms (public company) in July 2022, $4.5B deal. Not classic PE — this is a strategic JV between two ag conglomerates — but exhibits PE-like integration discipline.

**1. Commercial scope.** As of April 2026, **Aaron Leach was promoted to Chief Commercial and Supply Chain Officer**, reporting to CEO Kevin McDaniel. The CCSO role explicitly bundles sales, marketing, R&D, and supply chain into one C-suite seat. This is a notable structural choice — most platforms separate commercial from supply chain. **Confirmed** via April 2026 press release.

**2. Entity/brand × function matrix.** Two-channel split underneath the CCSO: **SVP Retail Sales (David Gadd)** and **SVP Commercial Sales (Randy Meyers, foodservice/institutional)**. This is channel-based, not entity-based. The Wayne and Sanderson commercial teams have been **fully integrated** by 2026, four years after merger close. Aaron Leach himself "led the integration of the Wayne Farms and Sanderson Farms merger" — meaning the architect of integration was promoted to run the integrated commercial function. **Confirmed.**

**3. Customer × category.** Retail vs. foodservice channel split is the primary cut. No evidence of preserved Wayne-only or Sanderson-only customer teams. The merger's commercial logic was always about **single counterparty to retailers and QSR chains**. **Inferred** from organizational design.

**4. The PE-style playbook.** Even though Continental Grain isn't pure PE, it operates with PE discipline. Pattern: 4-year integration window from close (2022) to fully unified C-suite (2026); appoint the integration leader to permanent commercial leadership (rewards delivery, signals end of integration phase); collapse commercial + supply chain into one seat (signals customer-back operating model). **Confirmed.**

**5. Synergy capture.** Plant network optimization, single-customer-team retailer leverage, foodservice cross-sell of Wayne and Sanderson capabilities. Not publicly quantified post-close (private company). **Inferred.**

**6. What broke.** No major public stumbles. The DOJ scrutinized the JV pre-close (lawmakers asked DOJ to examine it for antitrust); approval came with consent terms. Operationally, the four-year integration window suggests it took longer than a typical 100-day playbook anticipates — consistent with the complexity of integrating two large poultry organizations with different cultures and grower networks.

## Hometown Food Company (Brynwood Partners, 2018–present)

Brynwood acquired Pillsbury shelf-stable baking, Hungry Jack, Funfetti, White Lily, Jim Dandy, Arrowhead Mills, Sunspire, Martha White from J.M. Smucker in 2018 — an instant multi-brand portfolio in a single deal, not a sequential rollup.

**1. Commercial scope.** Tom Polke (ex-Ferrara Candy president) installed as President and CEO in 2020, replacing the original post-close team. ~300 employees, two plants (Toledo OH, Hereford TX). Small enough that there's no separate CCO published; commercial reports to the CEO. **Confirmed.**

**2. Entity/brand × function matrix.** With ~300 employees across 7+ brands, **fully integrated commercial function** is the only viable model. Brynwood's playbook is explicit: buy orphaned brands from strategics, run lean, take cost out, milk cash flow. No federation — there isn't enough scale to run dedicated brand teams. **Inferred** but high confidence.

**3. Customer × category.** Single sales team representing the full bakery-aisle portfolio at retailers. Brynwood specifically targets brands that share retail buyer relationships (baking aisle), so the integration thesis demands a unified team. **Inferred.**

**4. The PE playbook.** Brynwood's pattern across Hometown, Sunny Delight, Chef Boyardee (when they had it): acquire from a strategic that's neglecting the brand, install a lean team, run for cash, occasionally re-sell. Different from Advent's growth playbook on Sovos. This is **value PE, not growth PE.** **Confirmed** via Brynwood public materials and Food Dive coverage.

**5. Synergy capture.** Day-one cost takeout from corporate overhead the brands previously absorbed at Smucker. Selling-cost rationalization (one team replacing seven sub-team allocations). **Inferred.**

**6. What broke.** Limited public signal. The CEO change in 2020 (less than 2 years after close) suggests early-period turnover, but Polke has held the seat since.

## Other reference points (briefer)

- **Hostess (Apollo + Metropoulos, 2013–2016).** Bought brands and bakeries out of liquidation; rebuilt distribution from scratch with a new DSD-light model. Sold to Gores Holdings (SPAC) at ~10x return in three years. Lesson: a PE-backed restart can move faster than a multi-brand integration because there were no legacy commercial teams to merge.
- **Big Heart Pet Brands (KKR/Vestar/Centerview, 2011–2015).** Carved out of Del Monte's pet division. Ran for ~3 years as a standalone before selling to J.M. Smucker. The carve-out itself required building a standalone commercial team — meaning even within KKR's hold period the company *built* commercial capability, didn't *consolidate* it.
- **Utz Brands (public via SPAC, but operates with PE-style M&A).** Acquired Truco/On the Border in 2020 and explicitly preserved the Truco division leadership (Shane Chambers retained as division CEO) for 12+ months before integration. Gradual integration, not big-bang. Confirms the **federation-first, integrate-later** pattern is real.

## PE Playbook Patterns

Pulling across this set, six recognizable patterns emerge:

**1. Sales integrates first. Marketing/brand integrates last (or never).** This is the strongest cross-company signal. Sovos explicitly built one sales force across four brands while preserving brand-team marketing. Wayne-Sanderson collapsed to channel-based sales (retail / foodservice) within four years. Hometown ran integrated from day one (too small not to). Hearthside and Shearer's, being B2B/private label, didn't have brand teams to begin with. **Why:** retailer leverage is the easiest, fastest commercial synergy to capture; brand equity destruction is the biggest risk in CPG. So you consolidate the lever that captures synergy, preserve the asset that holds value.

**2. The "interim CCO" pattern is real but quiet.** Less visible than expected in these cases — most PE platforms install a permanent CCO/CSO at platform formation rather than running an interim. Sovos: Risa Cretella appointed CSO and stayed through IPO and Campbell sale. Wayne-Sanderson: Aaron Leach was *internal* integration leader, then promoted to CCSO (the integration leader becoming permanent CCO is itself a recognizable pattern). Hearthside: SVP-level commercial leaders rotated more often. **The pattern is less "interim CCO during integration" and more "integration leader becomes CCO once integration is judged complete."**

**3. The 100-day plan / year-1 / year-2 / year-3 cadence.**
- **Days 1–100:** Stabilize the acquired entity. Lock down customer relationships (no buyer should hear from you in chaos). Identify the few "no-regret" sales-force consolidations (e.g., one team at Walmart) and execute. Marketing untouched.
- **Year 1:** Consolidate sales force at major retailers. Build shared trade-spend infrastructure. Stand up shared category management capability. Brand teams stay dedicated.
- **Year 2:** Shared services (finance, IT, HR) consolidate fully. Begin cross-brand portfolio thinking at retailers (joint business plans, shared shelf strategy).
- **Year 3+:** Reassess: keep brand-dedicated marketing or consolidate marketing into category-based teams? Most platforms keep brand-dedicated marketing if brands are differentiated (Sovos), consolidate if they're not (Hometown).

**4. Federated structures get re-centralized when synergy targets slip.** Hearthside's 2023 downgrade is the cautionary tale — platform got too federated across plants/customers, couldn't show consolidated synergies, lenders punished it. Trigger for re-centralization is almost always a **financial event**: missed lender covenant, refinancing window, exit prep. Sovos re-centralized progressively because Advent had a clear IPO timeline; Hearthside's owners didn't have a forcing function until the credit downgrade.

**5. PE-specific levers vs. strategic-acquirer levers.**
- **PE levers:** Speed (90-day decision velocity vs. corporate's 9-month). Outsider CEO appointments (not internal promotes). Operating partner co-leadership (Advent's Bill Johnson at Sovos). Hold-period clarity (3–7 years means decisions can't slip). Debt-driven discipline.
- **Strategic-acquirer levers:** Existing customer relationships absorb the new brands (Campbell absorbing Sovos meant Sovos's sales force became redundant overnight). Distribution scale. Shared infrastructure already in place.
- **Implication:** PE platforms should not try to use strategic levers they don't have. The biggest mistake is a PE platform pretending it has Campbell-scale infrastructure when it doesn't.

**6. The "right number of brand presidents" question.** Sovos ran with 4 GM-led brand units at peak. Hometown runs with 0 (CEO covers all brands). Wayne-Sanderson runs with 0 (channel-based, not entity-based). The pattern: **you need brand presidents only when brands have differentiated equity and innovation programs that justify dedicated leadership.** If brands are commoditized or share customers heavily, brand presidents are dead weight.

## What the Teays River / GPG situation should learn from this set

GPG is consolidating five legacy entities (Grimmway, Cal-Organic, Bunny Luv, Country Sweet, others), most produce-focused, all sharing the same retail buyer base (produce buyers at Walmart, Kroger, Costco, foodservice carrot specifiers). The applicable patterns:

1. **Sales should integrate first, fast.** Produce buyers at major retailers don't want five Grimmway-family sellers calling on them. The single biggest commercial synergy is being one counterparty at the retailer's produce desk. This is the Sovos lesson and the Wayne-Sanderson lesson. Move on this in year one.

2. **Brand differentiation is much weaker in produce than in CPG, so federated brand teams are less defensible.** Cal-Organic has organic positioning; Bunny Luv has a kid/snack positioning; the rest are largely commodity-or-private-label-adjacent. Unlike Sovos (Rao's vs. noosa = genuinely different consumer franchises), GPG's portfolio doesn't justify four GM-led brand teams. Lean toward **integrated commercial function with category-based PMs** rather than brand-dedicated GMs.

3. **Channel split is the most defensible secondary cut.** Following Wayne-Sanderson: retail vs. foodservice (vs. ingredient/B2B if relevant) is a cleaner organizational line than entity-by-entity. Consider a CCO with retail-sales SVP and foodservice-sales SVP underneath, plus a category management / shopper insights leader, plus a marketing leader (lean, shared).

4. **Combining commercial + supply chain under one C-suite seat is increasingly the move in produce/protein.** Wayne-Sanderson's CCSO model recognizes that in commodity-adjacent agri-businesses, the customer conversation *is* the supply conversation (volumes, allocations, seasonality). GPG should consider whether its current org separates these in a way the integrated business won't justify.

5. **Don't fall into the Hearthside trap.** PE-backed scale without commercial discipline destroys value. Teays River will judge GPG on EBITDA and on the credibility of synergy capture. A federated five-entity structure two years post-consolidation will read to lenders the same way Hearthside's federated plant network read in 2023: unrealized synergies, downgraded credit.

6. **Time-box the integration window.** Wayne-Sanderson ran 4 years from close to fully unified C-suite — at the long end of acceptable. Sovos compressed faster because Advent had IPO discipline. Without an IPO timeline GPG should still set a 24–30 month internal forcing function: by then, one CCO, one sales force, one marketing team, one category management capability.

7. **Pick the integration leader carefully — they often become the permanent CCO.** Aaron Leach at Wayne-Sanderson, Risa Cretella at Sovos. Whoever runs GPG's commercial integration is the most likely candidate for permanent CCO. Choose accordingly: somebody with credibility across all five entities, not just the dominant one.
