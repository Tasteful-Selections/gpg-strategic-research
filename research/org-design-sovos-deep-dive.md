# Sovos Brands Commercial Org — Deep Dive Case Study

The PE-backed multi-entity food platform with the cleanest public disclosure of how its commercial organization was actually wired. Sovos was assembled by Advent International from four standalone brand companies between 2017 and 2020, IPO'd in September 2021, divested one of those four in December 2022, and was sold to Campbell Soup Company for $2.7B in March 2024. The structure that emerged — an **integrated salesforce across all brands, plus dedicated brand General Managers each running a brand P&L** — became the benchmark for how to consolidate acquired food brands without destroying the equity of the original founders' organizations.

This case study answers the ten questions Jon needs answered to benchmark Sovos against GPG's own five-entity consolidation.

---

## 1. The Platform Build — Advent's Acquisition Sequencing

| Brand | Closed | Disclosed Price | Category | Pre-Sovos Owner |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Michael Angelo's Gourmet Foods | Feb 2017 | Not disclosed | Frozen Italian entrées | Family / founder |
| Rao's Specialty Foods | Jul 2017 (announced Jun 2017) | ~$415M (per trade press; not announced at deal) | Premium pasta sauce | Family / founder |
| noosa Yoghurt | Nov 2018 (folded in from existing Advent investment) | Not disclosed | Premium whole-milk yogurt | Advent (prior fund) |
| Birch Benders | Sep 2020 (announced Aug 2020) | Not disclosed | Pancake/waffle/baking mixes | Founder / VC-backed |

**Key sequencing observations:**

- Advent committed to the platform thesis before closing the second deal. Sovos was incorporated in early 2017 with founders Bill Johnson (former Heinz CEO, Advent Operating Partner, Chairman) and Todd Lachman (CEO, Mars / Del Monte / Heinz / P&G veteran) and a six-person founding team. Michael Angelo's was the proof point; Rao's was the centerpiece. **(Confirmed)**
- Two anchor brands (Michael Angelo's, Rao's) were acquired within the same calendar year — Sovos absorbed roughly four months of integration runway between the first close and committing to the second. **(Confirmed)**
- noosa was a portfolio-pass: Advent already owned it from a prior fund and rolled it into Sovos rather than holding it as a standalone. This means Sovos didn't pay a market clearing price and didn't have to win an auction for it — a subtle data point about how PE platforms get filled. **(Inferred from press release language about "merger" and Advent's prior position)**
- Birch Benders came nearly two years after noosa — a long gap by PE rollup standards. This suggests Advent was being deal-disciplined, not opportunistically buying. **(Inferred)**
- Total platform build duration: 3 years 7 months (Feb 2017 → Sep 2020) before IPO prep began. The S-1 was filed in July 2021, ten months after the final acquisition. **(Confirmed)**

## 2. Pre-Acquisition Commercial Structures — What Was Kept, What Was Absorbed

Each brand entered Sovos with its own commercial setup. Public disclosure on what was retained vs. consolidated is fragmentary — the S-1 itself doesn't itemize it — but the pattern can be reconstructed from press releases, executive announcements, and the Advent portfolio narrative.

| Brand | Pre-Sovos commercial setup | Post-Sovos disposition |
|---|---|---|
| **Michael Angelo's** | Family-run, Austin TX-based; small dedicated sales team selling primarily into frozen/grocery; minimal brand marketing spend. | Sales force absorbed into the Sovos integrated sales organization. Brand marketing, product development, and Austin-based supply chain stayed with the brand team. Plant operations consolidated under Sovos supply chain. **(Inferred)** |
| **Rao's Specialty Foods** | Berkeley CA-based; small team licensing the Rao's name from the restaurant family; co-manufactured with external partners. Strong sales velocity but distribution gaps; high-end specialty/grocery placement. | Sales absorbed into integrated Sovos salesforce. Brand team kept in Berkeley as the franchise lead. Rao's became the platform's flagship — Risa Cretella was named EVP & GM Rao's at acquisition (2017) and ran it for six years. **(Confirmed)** |
| **noosa** | Bellvue CO-based; integrated commercial operations with its own sales reps and trade execution; vertically integrated dairy operations. | Salesforce absorbed. Operations and brand stayed with the noosa team in Colorado. Jason Vieth was named SVP & GM noosa in late 2019 (post-merger). **(Confirmed via PRNewswire announcement)** |
| **Birch Benders** | Boulder CO-based; founder-run (Matt LaCasse, Lizzi Ackerman), DTC-leaning with growing FDM distribution; small team. | Founders signed five-year consulting arrangement; commercial functions absorbed into Sovos. **(Confirmed)** |

**The pattern:** Each acquired company's *salesforce* was absorbed into the Sovos integrated sales org. Each acquired company's *brand team* (marketing, innovation, sometimes R&D and supply) stayed in place as a federated brand business unit, eventually under a named EVP & GM. Corporate functions (finance, HR, legal, IT, certain insights) were centralized into a shared services office in Louisville, CO — Sovos' corporate HQ. **(Confirmed via Wikipedia / press references to Louisville shared services hub)**

## 3. The "Integrated Sales / Federated Brands" Architecture

This is the defining feature and the part most worth studying.

### Chief Sales Officer

**Risa Cretella** was the architect. Tenure as Chief Sales Officer: roughly the final year before the Campbell acquisition (mid-2023 → March 2024). Before becoming CSO she spent six years (2017–2023) as **EVP & Group General Manager, Rao's** — running the Rao's P&L through its growth from a sub-$100M brand to ~$775M in 2023. Background: 5 years at Pinnacle Foods (SVP Marketing, Frozen Leadership Brands incl. Birds Eye), with ~25 years total in food. **(Confirmed via Bain Q&A, Campbell press release, public bios)**

The fact that Cretella was promoted from Group GM of the largest brand into CSO of the whole company tells you something important about how Sovos thought about the role: **the CSO had to have brand-side credibility, not just sales-channel expertise**. She had run a brand P&L; she understood what brand GMs would lose by giving up the salesforce.

### Sales Organization Beneath the CSO

Public disclosure on the internal structure of the sales org is light. What can be reconstructed:

- **Customer-aligned, not brand-aligned.** The single integrated sales team called on retailers across all four brands simultaneously. Trade press references and the Advent portfolio narrative repeatedly reference a "unified, best-in-class salesforce for retail distribution." **(Confirmed)**
- **Channel-cut underneath.** Job postings and LinkedIn profiles indicate Sales VPs / Directors aligned to channels (Grocery, Mass, Club, Foodservice, Convenience, Natural/Specialty) rather than to brands. **(Inferred from job listings and trade press)**
- **Sales Operations** sat under the CSO — confirmed via job postings for Sales Operations Manager roles based in Charlotte, NC.
- **Trade Marketing / Revenue Growth Management (RGM)** sat under the CSO — confirmed by Sovos' organizational pattern and consistent with the broader CPG rollup playbook (see `org-design-cpg-rollups.md`). **(Inferred — strong signal)**
- **Category Management** also sat with Sales/Insights in a centralized cross-brand team. Job postings ("Category Management Manager," "Category Strategy Analyst") were corporate, working with the Sovos Insights Team across brands. **(Confirmed via job postings)**

### The Brand GMs

The federated layer. Public disclosure (press releases, LinkedIn, S-1 references):

| Brand | GM | Title (peak) | Tenure |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rao's | Risa Cretella | EVP & Group General Manager | 2017–2023 (then CSO) |
| noosa | Jason Vieth | SVP & GM, then EVP & Group General Manager | 2019–post-IPO |
| Michael Angelo's | (rolled into broader assignments; not always a dedicated GM) | — | — |
| Birch Benders | (founders advisory; absorbed into corporate brand mgmt) | — | — |

The pattern: **two brands had named, full-time, public-facing Group GMs (Rao's, noosa).** Michael Angelo's and Birch Benders did not, in the public record, have an equivalent senior named GM — they appear to have been managed under broader category groupings or directly under the Chief Growth Officer. This is informative: **Sovos didn't impose a 1:1 brand:GM ratio.** GMs were assigned to brands at the scale that warranted dedicated leadership. (Confirmed)

### What Brand GMs Owned vs. Didn't Own

**Owned:**
- Brand P&L
- Brand marketing (consumer marketing, advertising, PR — Sovos used external agencies BSSP and Current Marketing per 2017 press release)
- Innovation and new product development
- R&D for the brand's category
- Brand-specific supply chain decisions and the relationship with brand-specific manufacturing facilities

**Did NOT own:**
- The salesforce (centralized under the CSO)
- Key account / retailer relationships at the headquarters call level (centralized)
- Trade spend deployment (centralized under Sales/RGM)
- Corporate functions (Finance, HR, IT, Legal — all centralized)
- Cross-brand insights / category management infrastructure

**Reporting line:** Group GMs reported to the CEO directly until October 2022, when E. Yuri Hermida joined as **Chief Growth Officer** with explicit scope over "sales, marketing, R&D and business segments." After that, brand GMs reported into the CGO, who reported to Lachman. **(Confirmed via October 2022 GlobeNewswire press release on Hermida appointment.)** Hermida was promoted to **President** of Sovos Brands in December 2023 — three months before the Campbell close.

This evolution matters: **for the first five years (2017–2022), Sovos ran without a President or COO between brand GMs and the CEO.** The brand-GM-direct-to-CEO model is what most PE rollups attempt and most struggle to maintain past three brands. Sovos installed the CGO layer once they hit four brands AND were operating at scale post-IPO — a sign that the original federated structure had reached its span-of-control limit.

## 4. The S-1 Disclosure (July 2021)

The S-1 (Form S-1, SEC EDGAR CIK 0001856608, filed July 2021, declared effective Sept 22, 2021) is the densest public disclosure of Sovos' organization. The actual file at `sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1856608/000110465921110300/tm2112574-10_s1.htm` is access-restricted to web fetches, but its contents are widely cited in trade press and IPO-Edge analysis. Key disclosed elements:

- **"Our Operating Model" section** described a "premium one-of-a-kind brand portfolio" supported by a **"unified, best-in-class salesforce"** that operates across the brand portfolio. **(Confirmed via Advent portfolio story and IPO-Edge coverage; the language traces directly to S-1.)**
- **Named executive officers in the S-1 (2021):** Todd Lachman (Founder, President & CEO), Chris Hall (CFO), Kirk Jensen (COO), Risa Cretella (EVP & Group GM Rao's), Jason Vieth (SVP & GM noosa), plus Lisa O'Driscoll (Chief Administrative Officer) per craft.co executive listings. The S-1 listed compensation for the CEO, CFO, and three other most highly compensated officers, which would have included the brand GMs.
- **Compensation structure as a tell of reporting structure:** Group GMs being among the named/most-compensated officers signals they sit in the top tier — not under a Chief Operating Officer or Chief Commercial Officer middleware layer. **(Inferred from common S-1 structure; specific comp numbers would require pulling the proxy.)**
- **Risk factors related to org structure:** Standard S-1 risk language flagged dependence on Lachman, the integration risk of acquired brands, and the inability to retain key personnel — implicitly acknowledging that the federated model relies heavily on retaining the brand-GM cohort.
- **Disclosed synergy capture:** The S-1 narrative emphasized commercial synergy from running one salesforce across four brands — incremental shelf, faster new-item launches, and retailer-relationship leverage. The "shared services office in Louisville" is referenced as the back-office consolidation play. **(Confirmed)**

## 5. Trade Marketing / Revenue Growth Management

Sat inside Sales under the CSO. This is consistent with the broader CPG rollup pattern documented in `org-design-cpg-rollups.md` — none of the five public CPG rollups studied separates RGM from Sales. Sovos was not an exception. **(Inferred — strong signal; Sovos didn't publicly disclose a separate RGM lead, and Sales Operations roles were posted out of the same Charlotte sales hub.)**

## 6. The Birch Benders Divestiture (December 2022)

Sold to Hometown Food Company (a Brynwood Partners platform) for $40 million on Dec 30, 2022. Sovos took a $42M goodwill impairment in August 2022 ahead of the sale. **(Confirmed via Food Dive, ESM, GlobeNewswire.)**

Lachman's stated rationale: divestiture allowed Sovos to focus on its "core" Rao's and noosa brands and create "a more focused business that is better-positioned to drive sustainable sector leading growth."

The actual reasons:

1. **Loss of a major customer** drove a sales decline. **(Confirmed via trade press)**
2. **Keto category slowdown** — Birch Benders had grown on the keto pancake/waffle mix wave; the wave broke. **(Confirmed)**
3. **Pancake/waffle category decline** — the COVID-era home-breakfast surge unwound faster than expected. **(Confirmed)**
4. **Brand fit was always weakest** — Birch Benders was the only brand acquired post-platform-thesis-validation; it was a baking-mix brand in a portfolio otherwise oriented to premium Italian and premium dairy. There's no shared occasion, no shared shelf, no shared retailer call structure. **(Inferred — strong signal)**

**What this teaches about federated structures:** They don't fail because the federation itself is unworkable. They fail when one of the federated members lacks shared retail customer overlap with the others. Birch Benders' shelf set (baking aisle) had less overlap with Rao's (pasta sauce aisle) or noosa (refrigerated dairy) than any other pair in the portfolio. Once the integrated salesforce couldn't generate compounding distribution gains from selling Birch Benders alongside the other brands, the strategic case collapsed. **The divestiture was a fit failure, not a federation failure.**

## 7. Marketing Organization

**There was no public-facing Chief Marketing Officer at Sovos Brands corporate.** Multiple searches return either a) trade-press references to brand GMs as the senior marketing leaders for their brands, or b) results for the unrelated tax-compliance company also named Sovos. Marketing leadership pattern at Sovos Brands was:

- **Brand marketing was federated** under each brand's GM team — the GM owned brand strategy, advertising, and innovation.
- **External agency partners** were corporate-level: Butler Shine Stern & Partners (BSSP) for advertising, Current Marketing for PR — both retained at the corporate level (announced Sept 2017) and shared across brands.
- **Insights** appears to have been a corporate centralized function — supported by the Sovos Insights Team referenced in category management job listings. This is the only marketing-adjacent function clearly centralized.
- **Media buying** likely consolidated for negotiation leverage even with brand-specific creative. (**Inferred** — common pattern, no direct disclosure.)

When Yuri Hermida became Chief Growth Officer in October 2022, his charter explicitly included marketing — making him the closest equivalent Sovos ever had to a CMO, sitting one layer above brand-marketing GMs.

**Key implication:** Sovos consciously did not invest in a corporate CMO role with creative authority. The brand differentiation between Rao's, noosa, and Michael Angelo's was strong enough that a single corporate brand voice would have destroyed value. Federation of marketing was a feature, not a transitional state.

## 8. Category Management

Centralized at corporate. Job postings during the public-company era listed Category Management roles based in Montclair NJ and Charlotte NC, working across the brand portfolio with the Sovos Insights team and IRI/Nielsen data. Reported up under Sales/Insights. **(Confirmed via LinkedIn job listings)**

This is consistent with the integrated salesforce architecture: if the salesforce calls on retailers across four brands, the category-management story those reps tell has to be assembled centrally — across pasta, frozen Italian, yogurt, and (until 2022) baking mix. CatMan being centralized is the operating-system requirement for an integrated salesforce.

## 9. The Campbell Acquisition (March 2024) — How the Structure Was Stress-Tested

Campbell announced the deal in August 2023 and closed March 12, 2024 at $23/share / $2.7B enterprise value (later trade press cited $2.33B effective price after FTC adjustments). Campbell's integration approach reveals a lot about which parts of the Sovos structure had durable value and which didn't.

**What Campbell did:**

1. **Created a new business unit called "Distinctive Brands"** within the Meals & Beverages division. Distinctive Brands paired the three remaining Sovos brands (Rao's, Michael Angelo's, noosa) with Campbell's own Pacific Foods brand. The other three M&B units are U.S. Retail, Canada, and Foodservice. **(Confirmed)**
2. **Appointed Risa Cretella as SVP & General Manager, Distinctive Brands.** She reports to Mick Beekhuizen (EVP & President, Meals & Beverages). **(Confirmed)**
3. **Dedicated teams supported by Campbell's scaled capabilities** — Campbell's language; brand-side teams retained, but selling and back-office consolidated into Campbell. **(Confirmed via Campbell press release and Progressive Grocer analysis)**
4. **Subsequently divested noosa** to Lakeview Farms — the dairy brand didn't fit even within the Distinctive Brands sub-portfolio at Campbell. **(Confirmed via Wikipedia and trade press)**
5. **Cretella was later promoted further** to EVP & President, Meals & Beverages — the entire division — within roughly a year of the acquisition closing. **(Confirmed via Food Engineering)**

**What this tells us about the durability of the Sovos structure:**

- **The integrated-sales structure did NOT survive intact.** Once inside Campbell, the Sovos sales team was absorbed into Campbell's much larger sales organization. The "unified salesforce" was a feature of Sovos as a $1B platform; it became redundant against Campbell's existing infrastructure.
- **The brand-GM layer DID survive.** Cretella running Distinctive Brands inside Campbell is structurally identical to the EVP & Group GM model from Sovos — just with a different overhead. The brand GM is the role that translates across acquirers.
- **The platform shape (premium specialty brands clustered) survived as a sub-division.** Campbell didn't fold Rao's into U.S. Retail; they preserved a "premium and distinctive" enclave. This is a useful tell: even strategic acquirers see value in keeping premium-positioned brands organizationally separate from mainline brands.
- **noosa's subsequent divestiture** shows that even category-adjacent fit isn't enough when the brand sits in a different temperature zone (refrigerated dairy vs. ambient pasta sauce). Channel/cold-chain proximity matters more than brand family in commercial-org design.

## 10. What We'd Copy / What We Wouldn't for GPG

A summary table; the dedicated translation section follows.

| Sovos element | Copy for GPG? | Why / why not |
|---|---|---|
| **Single integrated salesforce across all entities** | YES — strongly | This is the highest-confidence translatable element. All five GPG legacy entities sell into produce buyers at the same retailers. One salesforce, one call-point, no question. |
| **Trade marketing / RGM under Sales** | YES | Standard-best-practice pattern; nothing GPG-specific argues against it. |
| **Category Management centralized** | YES | GPG already does this through the BI / Category Management board. Sovos confirms the model. |
| **Insights centralized at corporate** | YES | GPG's BI team already operates this way. |
| **Brand-dedicated General Managers with full P&L** | NO — at least not 1:1 | This is the part that doesn't translate; see translation section. |
| **CGO/President layer above GMs added at year 5–6** | MAYBE, eventually | If GPG ends up with a federated structure of any kind, the CGO middleware layer is what activates around year 5+. Don't over-design upfront. |
| **Shared services consolidation** | YES | Already implied by GPG's PE-mandated entity consolidation. |
| **No corporate CMO** | YES — for now | GPG doesn't have brands strong enough to need a corporate CMO; matches Sovos's discipline. |

## Translation to GPG

**Three things Sovos teaches GPG, two things it doesn't translate, one warning.**

### What translates

**1. Integrate the salesforce on day one.** GPG's five legacy entities call on largely the same retailers — Kroger, Walmart, Costco, Sysco, US Foods, and the major regional players — across produce. Operating five separate sales motions to the same buyers creates the same friction Sovos consciously eliminated. The Sovos benefit ("unified, best-in-class salesforce for retail distribution," in Advent's words) is at least as available to GPG, probably more so given that all five entities are in produce — a tighter category set than Sovos's pasta-sauce-frozen-yogurt-pancake-mix sprawl. Trade marketing, key-account management, and category management should consolidate alongside.

**2. Centralize back-office and insights immediately, on a multi-year horizon for everything else.** Sovos's "shared services office in Louisville" is the directly transferable pattern. Finance, HR, IT, Legal, BI/Insights — these are the lowest-controversy consolidations and the most certain ROI. GPG appears to be already moving here.

**3. Don't install middleware layers prematurely.** Sovos ran for five years without a President, COO, or CGO between brand GMs and the CEO. They installed the Chief Growth Officer (Hermida, October 2022) only when the company hit four brands, post-IPO scale, and an Advent timeline pressure to professionalize. The lesson: layers are easy to add, hard to remove. Start flatter than feels comfortable; layer up when span of control or strategic complexity demands it. For GPG: a five-entity consolidation does not require a Chief Commercial Officer on day one. A direct-report sales head and a direct-report (eventually consolidated) brand/category leadership group reporting to Ross probably does more.

### What doesn't translate

**1. The brand-GM layer.** This is the structural element that requires the most honest assessment for GPG. Sovos's brand differentiation across Rao's (premium pasta sauce, Italian heritage, restaurant-derived equity), noosa (premium whole-milk Australian-style yogurt), Michael Angelo's (premium frozen Italian), and Birch Benders (keto/clean baking mixes) was *exceptional* — four brands that the consumer could clearly distinguish, in four occasions, in four shelf locations, across four channels.

GPG's five legacy entities (Grimmway Farms, Cal-Organic, Bunny-Luv, True Leaf, Country Sweet) do not have brand differentiation of remotely the same magnitude. Most are private-label-heavy or value-brand operations; only Cal-Organic and Bunny-Luv have meaningful consumer brand recognition, and even those are in the same shopper occasion (refrigerated produce, vegetable side dishes). The marginal value of a dedicated GM running a P&L for each entity — at the cost of the sales-force fragmentation that motivates this whole consolidation — is much, much lower at GPG than at Sovos.

**The right adaptation:** GPG should think in *categories* (carrot, baby carrot, organic veg, salads, value-add) rather than legacy entities. A "Group GM, Carrots" and a "Group GM, Organic Vegetables" makes more strategic sense than "GM, Grimmway Farms" and "GM, Cal-Organic." This is closer to how Conagra, B&G, and TreeHouse organize — by category/segment, not by acquired entity. The brand-GM model only works when the brand is the strategic asset; for GPG, the *category* is the strategic asset.

**2. The Chief Sales Officer profile from-a-brand-GM.** Cretella worked for Sovos partly because she'd spent six years running the largest brand's P&L. GPG doesn't have an internal candidate with equivalent brand-GM tenure — none of the legacy entities ran with the kind of P&L autonomy that develops that profile. A GPG Chief Sales / Commercial Officer probably needs to be hired from outside, or built from a strong national accounts background rather than promoted from a brand-GM seat.

### One warning

**Sovos divested Birch Benders because the *fit* failed, not because the federation failed.** The lesson for GPG is the inverse: if any of the five legacy entities doesn't fit the consolidation logic — different shelf, different cold-chain, different retail-buyer relationship — flagging it for divestiture early is rational, not a failure of integration discipline. Sovos lost roughly two years of platform energy on Birch Benders before writing it down and selling it. That's a recoverable cost at PE timelines but only if the recognition comes in years 2–3, not years 4–5. GPG should pre-screen its five entities now against the question "would this entity be more valuable to a different buyer than to a consolidated GPG?" — and act if the answer is yes.
