# Wayne-Sanderson Farms Commercial Org Design Deep-Dive

**Why this case matters for GPG.** Wayne-Sanderson is the cleanest publicly-trackable analog to the Grimmway Produce Group / Teays River five-entity consolidation. It is multi-entity (Wayne Farms + Sanderson Farms, plus the bolt-on Harrison Poultry in 2025), commodity-adjacent ag, vertically integrated, private-and-PE-style backed (Continental Grain + Cargill JV), and the integration ran roughly four years from 2022 close to a 2026 reorg that crystallized the commercial structure. The April 2026 appointment of Aaron Leach as Chief Commercial AND Supply Chain Officer — a single bundled C-suite seat — is the most copyable structural reference for what GPG could land on at the end of its own consolidation.

**Confidence framing.** The deal mechanics, leadership appointments, channel mix, and reporting structure are largely **confirmed** through press releases, trade press, and a recent Food Processing 2025 Processor of the Year profile. The pre-merger Wayne Farms commercial organization is partially **inferred** because Wayne was private under Continental Grain and never had to disclose. The internal split between category management, trade marketing, and shopper insights is **speculative** beyond what can be deduced from the visible reporting line.

---

## 1. The Deal and Integration Sequence

**Announcement.** August 9, 2021 — Cargill and Continental Grain announce a 50/50 JV to acquire Sanderson Farms (then publicly traded) at $203/share, ~$4.5B enterprise value, and combine it with Wayne Farms (Continental Grain's existing poultry subsidiary). **Confirmed** (Cargill press release, Sanderson Farms 8-K).

**Close.** July 22, 2022 — deal closes after a roughly 11-month DOJ antitrust review that pushed the original target close (December 2021 / Q1 2022). The combined company is named Wayne-Sanderson Farms and headquartered at Wayne Farms' Oakwood, GA office. **Confirmed.**

**Day-one CEO.** Clint Rivers, then-CEO of Wayne Farms, becomes CEO of the combined entity. This is the standard Continental Grain pattern: the existing Wayne Farms leadership team is the spine, with Sanderson talent and assets folded in. Lampkin Butts (Sanderson's longtime President & COO) does not transition. **Confirmed.**

**Integration timeline — multi-year.** The integration was not a six-quarter sprint. Public signals indicate:

| Window | Milestone | Confidence |
|---|---|---|
| Jul 2022 – Apr 2023 | Day-one ops; Aaron Leach leads merger integration office | Confirmed |
| Apr 2023 | New senior leadership identified across business units | Confirmed (GlobeNewswire, 4/19/2023) |
| 2023 | Rebrand to unified Wayne-Sanderson Farms identity | Confirmed |
| Jun 2024 | David Gadd promoted to SVP & GM Retail Business Unit | Confirmed |
| Sep 2024 | Rivers retirement and McDaniel succession announced | Confirmed |
| Apr 1, 2025 | Kevin McDaniel becomes CEO; Rivers becomes Executive Chairman | Confirmed |
| Jul 2025 | Acquires Harrison Poultry (Golden Goodness brand) — first bolt-on | Confirmed |
| Apr 1, 2026 | Aaron Leach promoted to CCSO; Michael Crump to COO; Gadd, Meyers, Watkins named under Leach | Confirmed |

The reorg announced April 3, 2026 — almost exactly four years post-close — reads as the company declaring the integration substantively complete and locking in the steady-state commercial architecture. **Inferred but well-supported.**

**Who led integration.** Aaron Leach. Press release language is explicit: he "led the integration of the Wayne Farms and Sanderson Farms merger." His pre-merger role was Director of Strategic Planning, then Senior Director of Supply Chain, at Wayne Farms (he joined Wayne in 2005). He was not an outside integration consultant. He was the inside operator handed the integration mandate, kept it through the four-year run, and was rewarded with the bundled CCSO seat once the work was done. **Confirmed.**

**When did each commercial function consolidate.** Public visibility is partial:
- **Sales (channel sales force).** Likely consolidated by the 2023 leadership refresh; Gadd's 2024 SVP/GM Retail Business Unit promotion is the externally visible signal that the retail sales function was unified. **Inferred.**
- **Marketing.** Unified brand identity rolled out 2023; the 2023 Chicken Marketer of the Year award accepted by Hilary Burroughs (VP Marketing & Communication) suggests a consolidated marketing function was operational by 2023. **Inferred-to-confirmed.**
- **Category management / trade marketing / shopper insights.** No public visibility on standalone consolidation. Almost certainly absorbed into the Retail Business Unit and now reports up through Gadd. **Speculative.**
- **Supply chain.** Bundled with Commercial only at the April 2026 reorg. Before that, supply chain ran as a parallel SVP-level function reporting to the COO/CEO (Leach was SVP Supply Chain & GM Prepared Foods immediately prior). **Confirmed.**

## 2. The Pre-Merger Commercial Organizations

**Wayne Farms (private, Continental Grain subsidiary, ~$2.0–2.1B sales 2020–2021).**
- **Channel anchor: foodservice and industrial.** Wayne's customer book skewed heavily to large foodservice operators, QSR/casual dining chains, food manufacturers, and institutional buyers. "Wayne's deep customer relationships across the food service sector" is how Continental Grain framed the merger rationale.
- **Brand portfolio at merger:** Wayne Farms (parent), Platinum Harvest (premium fresh), Naked Truth (premium/no-antibiotics), Chef's Craft (gourmet cuts), Ladybird (small-bird, female, on-bone aged), plus prepared-foods sub-brands Buffaloos, Crispy Fliers, Fly'n Saucers.
- **Org structure.** As a private CGC sub, Wayne disclosed almost nothing. Inferred structure: a President/CEO (Clint Rivers post-2017), with VP-level leaders for sales, marketing, supply chain, operations, and a sales organization presumably split by channel (foodservice/industrial as the primary book; retail growing). No CCO title was visible publicly. **Inferred.**
- **Plant footprint:** 11 fresh and further-processed facilities, primarily Alabama, Georgia, and North Carolina, plus two prepared-foods plants in Decatur, AL.

**Sanderson Farms (public, NASDAQ: SAFM until July 2022, ~$4.8B sales FY2021).**
- **Channel anchor: retail and big-bird/foodservice.** Sanderson was the more retail-branded of the two, with strong tray-pack programs at grocery and a recognizable consumer brand built through years of marketing investment.
- **Org structure (from public proxies and 10-Ks before take-private).** Sanderson ran a flat top team:
  - Joe F. Sanderson Jr. — Chairman & CEO
  - Lampkin Butts — President & COO (joined as VP Sales 1996; rose through sales)
  - Mike Cockrell — CFO and Treasurer / General Counsel function
  - Tim Rigney — Treasurer/Secretary
  - Plus VP-level operations and sales heads
- **The signal in Butts' resume.** Sanderson's #2 and operating chief came up through sales — Butts was VP Sales 1996–2004, then COO 2004–2022. That tells you Sanderson's corporate culture treated commercial leadership and operating leadership as continuous, not separated. **Confirmed.**
- **Plant footprint:** 13 production complexes anchored in Mississippi, Texas, Georgia, North Carolina, with one prepared-foods plant in Flowood, MS.

**The cleanest framing of the pre-merger difference, from CEO McDaniel:** "Both were very good at what they did…making them complementary in products and geography." Wayne brought the foodservice book and prepared-foods strength; Sanderson brought the retail brand and tray-pack volume. The merger logic is closer to *channel complementarity* than to *cost takeout via duplicate elimination*. That distinction matters for how the commercial reorg landed. **Confirmed.**

## 3. The Bundled CCSO Role — Aaron Leach

**Announced.** April 3, 2026, effective April 1, 2026 (start of FY2027).

**Title.** Chief Commercial and Supply Chain Officer. One C-suite seat owning both commercial functions (sales, marketing, R&D) and supply chain.

**Reports to.** Kevin McDaniel, President and CEO.

**Direct reports (named in the announcement):**
- David Gadd — SVP, Retail Sales
- Randy Meyers — SVP, Commercial Sales (foodservice/industrial)
- Craig Watkins — VP, Supply Chain

**Functional scope.** Per the press release: "sales, marketing, research and development, and supply chain functions." That bundle is the headline. R&D inside the commercial seat is unusual but logical for a vertically integrated protein company where product development is the bridge between operations capability and customer programs.

**Prior role.** SVP, Supply Chain & General Manager, Prepared Foods Business Unit. Note that the role he was promoted *out of* was already a hybrid: a functional SVP title (Supply Chain) layered on a P&L title (GM Prepared Foods). Wayne-Sanderson seems comfortable with bundled leadership remits at the senior level — the CCSO move extends a pattern, not invents one. **Confirmed.**

**Tenure.** Joined Wayne Farms in 2005. Twenty-one years inside the company. Rose through Financial Planning → Strategic Planning → Senior Director of Supply Chain → SVP Supply Chain / GM Prepared Foods → CCSO. He is a finance-and-strategy-trained operator, not a sales careerist.

**Public rationale for the bundling.** The press release language is bland: "These promotions recognize the significant contributions Aaron and Michael have made to the organization and reflect our confidence in their leadership." There is no public statement explicitly explaining why commercial and supply chain were bundled. The rationale has to be inferred from the company's operating reality (Section 8 below).

## 4. The Two SVPs Underneath — Gadd and Meyers

**The channel split.** Wayne-Sanderson runs commercial as **two channels, one boss**:
- **Retail Sales** (Gadd) — grocery, mass, club; tray-pack and branded fresh; ~30%+ of mix when retail and prepared-foods retail are combined.
- **Commercial Sales** (Meyers) — foodservice, restaurant, industrial, institutional, export. The "Commercial" label is the company's choice; functionally this is foodservice/B2B.

This split mirrors the pre-merger channel anchors almost exactly. Sanderson's heritage was retail; Wayne's heritage was foodservice. Rather than blend them into a single sales force, Wayne-Sanderson preserved the two channel motions and put a single executive over both. The plants and supply chain are shared; the customer-facing organization is channel-dedicated. **Inferred from structure, confirmed by the channel labels.**

**David Gadd — SVP Retail Sales.**
- 27-year tenure starting at Sanderson in 1997 as a "Beginning Trainee."
- Promoted June 2024 to SVP & GM Retail Business Unit (a two-year-old role in the merged company).
- Re-titled April 1, 2026 to SVP Retail Sales reporting into Leach. The shift from "GM Retail Business Unit" to "SVP Retail Sales" is meaningful: it moves Retail from a P&L unit reporting to the CEO into a sales function reporting through the CCSO. The P&L is being consolidated upward into the CCSO seat. **Inferred but strongly suggested by the title change.**
- BA Biology, University of Mississippi.

**Randy Meyers — SVP Commercial Sales.**
- Less public history. Appointed in the same April 2026 announcement; no prior promotion press release found. Almost certainly an internal appointment and likely a Wayne Farms heritage executive, given that the foodservice/commercial book was Wayne's heritage strength. **Inferred.**

**Pre-existing or post-merger?** Both roles in their *current* form (SVP Retail Sales / SVP Commercial Sales reporting to a CCSO) are post-merger and specifically post-April-2026. Gadd held a similar-but-larger role (SVP & GM Retail Business Unit) from June 2024. Meyers' seat may have existed in some form (a foodservice/commercial sales lead) but is publicly visible only from April 2026.

## 5. The Integration-Leader-Becomes-CCO Pattern

This is the most strategically valuable observation in the entire case.

**The pattern.** When a complex multi-entity merger needs an inside operator to run the integration, that person ends up with the institutional knowledge of every customer, every plant, every system, every cultural fault line. If they execute well, the natural reward is a permanent C-level seat — and the seat that fits the integration leader's accumulated knowledge is the commercial seat, because commercial is where customer continuity, plant-customer assignment, and supply allocation all meet.

**Wayne-Sanderson is the cleanest documented instance.**
- Aaron Leach joins Wayne 2005. Strategy and finance background.
- Promoted to Senior Director of Supply Chain pre-merger.
- Tapped as integration leader for the 2022 Wayne-Sanderson combination.
- During and after integration, he is given a hybrid SVP Supply Chain / GM Prepared Foods role — i.e., kept close to both the operational and customer-facing sides.
- April 2026, with integration declared complete, he is promoted to CCSO with sales, marketing, R&D, and supply chain all under him.

The four-year window from 2022 close to 2026 CCSO appointment is consistent with how long large vertically-integrated mergers take to settle. The fact that the integration leader was the appointee — rather than an external hire or a heritage Sanderson sales executive — is the data point. **Confirmed.**

**Why this pattern works in commodity-adjacent ag.** Integration leaders in protein and produce mergers spend years inside three loops simultaneously: (1) which plant should supply which customer, (2) how do legacy contracts and pricing structures harmonize, (3) where does the system hit capacity. Those are exactly the three loops a CCO has to keep running in steady state. The integration job is, in effect, a long audition for the CCO seat. **Inferred from the pattern.**

## 6. Marketing Organization

**What we know.**
- Wayne-Sanderson Farms won 2023 Chicken Marketer of the Year (WATTPoultry); the award was accepted by Hilary Burroughs, VP of Marketing and Communication. That is the highest-titled marketing person publicly visible. **Confirmed.**
- No CMO title is publicly visible. The senior marketing function appears to top out at VP level and reports up through Leach (per the April 2026 announcement that "marketing" is one of the four functions under CCSO). **Confirmed for the reporting line; inferred for the absence of a CMO.**
- Brand portfolio post-merger has been consolidated on the master-brand side (Wayne-Sanderson Farms as corporate identity) but **federated on the consumer-facing brand side**: Wayne Farms, Sanderson Farms, Covington Farms, Naked Truth, Platinum Harvest, Chef's Craft, Ladybird, Buffaloos, Crispy Fliers, plus newly-acquired Golden Goodness from Harrison Poultry, all coexist. **Confirmed.**

**The structural read.** Wayne-Sanderson runs marketing as a **single integrated function with brand-portfolio management inside it, no CMO seat**, sitting underneath the CCSO. This is tighter than Sovos Brands' "federated brands, integrated sales" model and looks more like a CPG-style portfolio brand house with one marketing chief reporting through commercial. **Inferred.**

**What this implies about marketing maturity.** A company doing $7B+ in revenue without a dedicated CMO is making a deliberate choice. The choice reads as: marketing is downstream of supply (you can only market what plants can produce), so reporting marketing into the function that owns both customers *and* supply (CCSO) keeps trade-offs in one head. Same logic that drove the CCSO bundle in the first place.

## 7. Category Management, Trade Marketing, Shopper Insights

**Public visibility: near zero.** No press releases, no LinkedIn-visible org leaders, no trade press coverage of these functions specifically.

**Best inference.** Category management and shopper insights live inside the Retail Sales organization under Gadd, not as a separate function. Trade marketing/customer marketing similarly lives under Retail and Commercial Sales respectively, scaled to channel.

**Why the inference.** Wayne-Sanderson's revenue mix is heavily fresh (61%) and most of that is sold as commodity chicken into both retail and foodservice. The category management discipline that matters most in branded CPG (line reviews, planogram strategy, shopper marketing investment optimization) is meaningful but not central — most volume moves on a per-load basis. So a free-standing category function would be over-investment. **Speculative but consistent with the visible org.**

**What this means for GPG.** Wayne-Sanderson is *not* a useful benchmark for whether to build a corporate category management center of excellence in fresh produce. GPG's category position with Walmart, Kroger, Costco is more brand-and-shelf-strategy intensive than commodity poultry's. Look elsewhere (CPG rollups, see companion file `org-design-cpg-rollups.md`) for the catman benchmark.

## 8. Why Bundle Commercial AND Supply Chain Into One C-Suite Seat

There is no public statement from Wayne-Sanderson's CEO or board explaining the rationale. The reasoning has to be reverse-engineered from the operating reality. Three converging reasons:

**8.1 The customer conversation IS the supply conversation.** In commodity-adjacent ag — poultry, beef, produce — every conversation with a major retail or foodservice customer is simultaneously about volume, allocation, seasonality, and price. A Walmart buyer asking for an extra million pounds in Q3 is asking about plant capacity, grow-out cycles, hatching schedules, and feed costs in one breath. Splitting commercial and supply chain into two C-level seats forces every major customer decision through a two-person consensus or a CEO escalation. Bundling them lets one executive answer in the room.

**8.2 The integration completed an organizational learning.** Wayne and Sanderson ran with separate commercial and supply chain leaders pre-merger. The integration era (2022–2026) — under Leach, who already held the hybrid Supply Chain SVP / GM Prepared Foods role — showed leadership the bundled motion worked. The April 2026 reorg formalized the operating model that had emerged during integration. **Inferred but strongly supported by the role-title progression.**

**8.3 P&L gravity sits in the bundle.** With Gadd and Meyers reporting to Leach as channel sales heads (rather than as Business Unit GMs with full P&L), the only person with combined visibility into channel revenue *and* unit cost is Leach. That makes him the de facto chief revenue officer with cost-of-goods authority — closer to a COO/CCO hybrid than a traditional CCO. The CEO (McDaniel) is left to focus on capital allocation, M&A integration, board relations, and live operations through Crump (COO). **Inferred.**

**The scarce-talent read.** Bundling commercial and supply chain means you only need to hire (or develop) one elite executive instead of two. For private companies that can't draw talent with public-equity comp packages, this is a meaningful constraint and probably a real driver. **Speculative but plausible.**

## 9. What's Federated vs. Centralized

**Centralized (post-2026 reorg):**
- Sales (one CCSO seat, two channel SVPs underneath)
- Marketing (one VP-level head reporting up through CCSO)
- Supply chain planning (Watkins under Leach)
- R&D / innovation (under Leach)
- Live operations (under Crump as COO across all 24 facilities)
- Engineering (under Crump)
- Finance, HR, Legal (corporate functions)
- Brand identity at the master-brand level

**Federated:**
- Consumer brand portfolio. Wayne Farms, Sanderson Farms, Covington Farms, Platinum Harvest, Naked Truth, Chef's Craft, Ladybird, Golden Goodness all preserved as distinct shelf brands. Master brand is corporate; consumer brands are the choice set.
- Plant identity. The 24 complexes still trade as legacy facilities locally; press coverage frequently references plants by their pre-merger names.
- Local community / grower relationships. The 2,000-family-farmer grower network is locally managed by complex, not nationally consolidated.

**Plant-customer assignment.** No public visibility on the specific allocation logic. Inferred best practice: customer-of-record sits with the SVP channel head; plant-of-supply is determined by the supply chain function (Watkins) based on geography, product capability (big-bird vs. small-bird, fresh vs. prepared), and capacity. The CCSO seat owning both means routine plant-customer reassignments don't require CEO escalation. **Speculative.**

## 10. Translation to GPG — What Copies, What Doesn't

### What translates well

**The bundled CCSO seat.** GPG operates in commodity-adjacent fresh produce where the customer conversation is the supply conversation — even more acutely than poultry, given fresh produce's shorter shelf life and weather-driven supply variability. A single GPG executive owning sales, marketing, customer development, and supply/allocation makes the customer call answerable in real time. The Wayne-Sanderson CCSO model is the structural template. **High translation value.**

**Two SVP channel heads under the CCO.** Mapping Gadd and Meyers onto GPG: split into SVP Retail (grocery, mass, club, e-commerce) and SVP Foodservice/Industrial (foodservice distribution, processors, ingredient sales, export). Both report to a GPG CCO. The two-channel-one-boss model preserves channel-specific go-to-market motions while consolidating the executive layer. **High translation value.**

**The integration-leader-becomes-CCO pattern.** If GPG already has an internal operator running the five-entity consolidation, that person is the leading internal candidate for the eventual CCO seat. Ross (CCO) and Jon should be tracking who is accumulating the cross-entity institutional knowledge during the consolidation, because the four-year Wayne-Sanderson arc says that person becomes the natural permanent commercial chief. **Direct strategic implication.**

**Federated brands, integrated sales.** Wayne-Sanderson preserves 8+ consumer brands but sells through one organization. GPG's legacy brands (Bunny-Luv, Cal-Organic, Grimmway, Country Sweet, etc., depending on which legacy entities are in scope) can be similarly preserved at the shelf while sales and marketing consolidate. This pattern is also visible in Sovos Brands and is likely the right answer for any multi-brand consumer rollup. **High translation value.**

**Multi-year integration timeline.** Four years from close to "integration complete" reorg. GPG should not expect or signal a faster timeline to its PE owner without an explicit case for why GPG's situation is structurally simpler (smaller asset base, fewer geographies, more brand similarity). **Direct planning implication.**

### What doesn't translate

**Single-protein simplicity.** Wayne-Sanderson is a pure-play chicken company. Every plant makes some form of chicken. GPG produces carrots, organic vegetables, potatoes, citrus, and other fresh items across multiple legacy entities. The plant-customer-assignment problem at GPG is multi-commodity and harder. The CCSO seat at GPG may need a stronger commodity-portfolio lieutenant structure than Wayne-Sanderson's single VP Supply Chain.

**Two-channel cleanliness.** Wayne-Sanderson's channel split (retail / commercial-foodservice) is unusually clean because heritage Wayne was foodservice and heritage Sanderson was retail. GPG's legacy entities have more overlapping retail relationships and the channel split may not fall as cleanly into two SVP seats. GPG may need three SVP channel heads (Retail / Foodservice / Ingredient-Industrial) or a region-based split rather than channel-based.

**No CMO model.** Wayne-Sanderson runs without a CMO and may be appropriate for a company where 61% of mix is commodity fresh. GPG's branded categories (Bunny-Luv, Cal-Organic) carry more shelf-marketing intensity per pound and may justify a CMO-level seat that Wayne-Sanderson doesn't have. Don't over-copy the absence-of-CMO.

**The sequencing of "integration complete" signaling.** Wayne-Sanderson did the bundled CCSO appointment as the *capstone* of integration. GPG should consider whether to lock in the CCSO architecture earlier — at the beginning of consolidation rather than the end — given that GPG has the benefit of having seen the Wayne-Sanderson playbook. Adopting the steady-state architecture from day one and growing into it may be cleaner than running parallel structures for four years and then collapsing them.

**Lessons from the Sanderson side that GPG should hold.** Sanderson's #2 (Lampkin Butts) came up through sales and ran as President & COO. He didn't transition into Wayne-Sanderson. That suggests the merged-company architecture was set by Continental Grain / Wayne, not negotiated as a peer. For GPG's five-entity consolidation, the architecture is being set by Teays River and the Grimmway leadership team — entity-leader continuity at the top of commercial should not be assumed.
