# Cross-Industry Org Design — Beverages and Hospitality Benchmarks

**Why this case matters for GPG.** Food/ag/CPG benchmarks have been documented elsewhere. This file looks beyond food at two industries with structural problems closer to GPG's than they first appear: **beverages** (multi-brand portfolios with vertical integration, where commercial must coordinate with owned production) and **hospitality** (multi-brand portfolios where a single corporate sales force calls on a single corporate buyer with 25–30 brand options to pitch). Hospitality is the closest direct structural analog to GPG-at-Walmart — five entities/categories sold through one buyer relationship.

**Confidence framing.** Public companies (Constellation, Diageo, Brown-Forman, Pernod Ricard, Marriott, Hilton, IHG) are largely **confirmed** through 10-Ks, press releases, leadership pages, and investor day materials. E&J Gallo is private; structural detail is **inferred** from job postings, press, and trade reporting. The internal mechanics of how the brand-house / commercial-house interface actually runs day-to-day is **speculative** beyond what shows up in titles and press releases.

## 1. Constellation Brands (NYSE: STZ)

**Top of house.** Bill Newlands (President & CEO 2019–April 2026) succeeded by Nicholas Fink (former CEO of Fortune Brands Innovations) effective April 13, 2026. Two division Presidents sit under the CEO — **Jim Sabia, EVP & President Beer Division** (Modelo, Corona, Pacífico) and **Sam Glaetzer, EVP & President Wine & Spirits Division** (Robert Mondavi, Kim Crawford, The Prisoner, Casa Noble). **Confirmed.**

**Brand × commercial matrix.** Constellation runs a **division-as-brand-portfolio** model. Each Division President is effectively a P&L owner with their own commercial stack underneath. Beer Division has its own:
- **Chief Sales Officer (Jack Edwards, SVP, effective May 2026)** — runs the national field sales organization. Edwards came from 20 years at Diageo. **Confirmed.**
- **Chief Marketing Officer (Matt Lindsay, SVP)** — runs Brand Marketing, Field/Lifestyle/Experiential Marketing, Trade Marketing, and Marketing Enablement under one umbrella. Note: trade marketing sits inside marketing, not sales. **Confirmed.**
- **SVP Brand Activation (Rene Ramos)** — owns field activation and trade marketing execution. **Confirmed.**

Wine & Spirits Division mirrors this with a **Jim Casey, SVP & Chief Sales Officer** running U.S. wholesale commercial. **Confirmed.**

**Vertical integration handling.** Constellation owns brewing capacity in Mexico (Nava, Obregón, expanding Veracruz) and California winery assets. Supply chain reports separately at the corporate level — **not bundled with commercial** at the division level. The Division President is the integrator between supply and commercial; Constellation has not adopted a Wayne-Sanderson-style CCSO bundle. **Confirmed.**

**CatMan / RGM placement.** Not visible in public org. Inferred to sit inside the Beer Division's commercial stack, likely under the CSO or in a separate Insights & Analytics function under the Division President.

**Recent reorgs.** January 2024 wine & spirits leadership refresh; April 2026 CEO transition with Beer Division CSO appointment. Constellation has been actively pruning the wine portfolio (sold most lower-tier wine brands to The Wine Group in 2021) to refocus on premium — which has shaped the divisional commercial model around fewer, bigger brands.

**Distinctively GOOD for GPG.** The **division-as-brand-portfolio** model with its own CSO + CMO + activation lead under a Division President is a clean way to handle multiple categories that share a customer base but need distinct brand stewardship. Each division can run as a self-contained commercial unit while corporate handles supply and capital.

**Cautionary tale.** The wine & spirits division has been a chronic underperformer — the divisional model can ossify if one division doesn't get the same operator attention. PE-backed GPG should set explicit P&L thresholds for keeping a division as its own commercial unit vs. folding it in.

## 2. Diageo (LSE: DGE / NYSE: DEO)

**Top of house.** Debra Crew (CEO 2023–July 2025) departed by mutual agreement; Nik Jhangiani (CFO) is interim CEO. Executive Committee includes EVP Global Brands, EVP Global Markets, North America CEO, and Global Director of Operations. **Confirmed.**

**Brand × commercial matrix — the brand-house / commercial-house split.** Diageo runs the cleanest version of this pattern at scale:
- **EVP Global Brands** (currently Philippe Guettat) owns brand strategy, brand equity, innovation pipelines, global creative across Johnnie Walker, Smirnoff, Captain Morgan, Guinness, Tanqueray, Don Julio, etc. **Confirmed.**
- **EVP Global Markets** (currently Gilles Bogaert) owns regional commercial execution — North America, Europe, LATAC, APAC, Africa. **Confirmed.**
- **Regional Markets** then have their own commercial leadership (NAM CEO, etc.) running the in-market sales teams.

Brand teams own equity, marketing creative, and innovation. Market teams own customer relationships, trade execution, and P&L. The brand teams influence; the market teams sell. **Confirmed pattern, inferred mechanics.**

**Vertical integration handling.** Diageo owns 30+ distilleries, packaging plants, and warehouses. **Global Director of Operations** sits separately on the ExCo — supply chain is NOT bundled into commercial. The CEO is the supply-commercial integrator.

**RGM placement — important.** Diageo has built a sophisticated **Revenue Growth & Planning** function with RGM Managers in every market. Public job postings describe these roles as accountable for "all RGM and GM levers across pack, channel, price and promotion, working closely with marketing, commercial and finance directors." RGM reports through **commercial / market leadership, NOT through brand**. This is the placement choice GPG should study most carefully. **Confirmed via job posting language.**

**CatMan placement.** Inside commercial, in-market — embedded with sales rather than separated as a standalone insights function.

**Strategy translation function.** The "Revenue Growth & Planning" team is itself the strategy translation layer — it sits between brand strategy (from Global Brands) and customer execution (from market sales) and translates one into the other. This is the closest beverage analog to a "Cat Strategy" function at GPG.

**Recent reorgs.** July 2025 CEO departure; $500M cost-cut plan announced May 2025 with substantial asset sales by 2028. Crew's tenure was marred by the Latin America inventory blowup — a textbook failure of the brand/commercial interface where brand teams forecast demand and commercial teams over-shipped.

**Distinctively GOOD for GPG.** The RGM-inside-commercial pattern with an explicit Revenue Growth & Planning function. This is the muscle GPG would need to translate brand-level pricing/portfolio thinking into customer-level execution. Diageo's failure mode is also instructive: when RGM is decoupled from real-time channel feedback, you over-ship.

**Cautionary tale.** The brand-house / commercial-house split creates accountability ambiguity when things go wrong — see Latin America. Whoever runs the integration (CEO at Diageo) has to actually integrate, not just chair meetings.

## 3. Brown-Forman (NYSE: BF.B)

**Top of house.** Lawson Whiting, President & CEO. January 2025 restructuring announced 12% workforce reduction, $70–80M annualized cost savings, Louisville Cooperage closure. **Confirmed.**

**Brand × commercial matrix.** Post-January 2025 restructuring:
- **Michael Masick, EVP & President, Americas** — owns USA, Canada, Mexico, Central America, Caribbean. USA & Canada were added to his prior LATAC remit. **Confirmed.**
- **Yiannis Pafilis, EVP & President, Europe / Africa / Asia Pacific** — added Africa, APAC, and global travel retail to prior Europe role. **Confirmed.**
- **Jeremy Shepherd, Chief Marketing Officer** — moved from leading USA & Canada commercial to global CMO. Brand house. **Confirmed.**

Two-region commercial structure (Americas / Rest-of-World) with one global CMO running brand strategy on top. Jack Daniel's family is ~60% of US retail value, so brand strategy is heavily Jack-anchored.

**Vertical integration handling.** Brown-Forman owns distilleries, cooperage (until 2025), bottling. May 2025 announcement of "Significant Evolution in U.S. Distribution" — they restructured wholesale distributor relationships in parallel with the org restructure. Supply chain reports separately; commercial owns the distributor interface. **Confirmed.**

**Sales force structure.** Brown-Forman sells through the three-tier system — they don't have a direct retail sales force in most markets. The "commercial" function is really a distributor management function in the US plus direct sales in Travel Retail and certain control states. This is structurally different from GPG, which sells direct to retail.

**CatMan placement.** Inferred to sit inside the regional commercial structure, region-by-region.

**Recent reorgs.** January 2025 — major. Trade tensions with Canada (April 2026, 61% sales decline) prompted further distributor changes in control states.

**Distinctively GOOD for GPG.** The simplification from country-by-country commercial structure to **two mega-regions (Americas / Rest-of-World) plus one global CMO** is exactly the consolidation move GPG is doing — going from five entity-by-entity commercial structures to one. Brown-Forman's choice to fold geography UP (not down) is the right direction of travel.

**Cautionary tale.** They did this DURING a sales decline, which compounded execution risk. GPG should ideally land its consolidation while market conditions are stable, not during a crisis. Also: Brown-Forman's heavy dependence on Jack Daniel's means brand strategy and commercial strategy are de facto fused around one brand — GPG with five categories doesn't have that simplifying advantage.

## 4. Pernod Ricard (Euronext: RI)

**Top of house.** Alexandre Ricard, Chairman & CEO. Pernod Ricard USA reports to **Conor McQuaid, Chairman & CEO Pernod Ricard North America**. **Confirmed.**

**Brand × commercial matrix.** Pernod runs a similar Brands / Markets split to Diageo:
- **EVP Global Brands** — house of brands (Absolut, Jameson, Chivas Regal, Martell, Havana Club).
- **EVP Global Markets** — regional commercial.
- **Regional CEOs** (NA, etc.) own P&L.
- **Regional CCO** — Paul Basford, CCO Pernod Ricard USA (joined 2024 from William Grant & Sons), reports to McQuaid. Owns "commercial planning, execution and transformation" for US wholesale. **Confirmed.**
- **Regional CMO** — Colin Kavanagh, CMO Pernod Ricard North America (since 2023). **Confirmed.**

**"Tomorrow 2" reorg (announced June 2025).** Pernod is reorganizing into **two brand divisions: Gold and Crystal**. Gold = champagne, Martell, Jameson. Crystal = Havana Club, Absolut, French aperitifs. €1B cost savings target by 2029. Bundles administrative tasks rather than per-brand. **Confirmed.**

**Vertical integration.** Owns distilleries, vineyards (Champagne, cognac), bottling. Operations report separately at corporate.

**Distinctively GOOD for GPG.** The Tomorrow 2 move — clustering brands into **two portfolios with shared back-office and shared commercial** rather than per-brand operations — is the consolidation play. Pernod's signal is that even at scale, you don't need five brand teams; you can cluster.

**Cautionary tale.** Pernod is doing this as a reaction to a 4% sales decline and China/India disruption. The cost-out logic was external; the strategic logic of brand clustering is unproven yet.

## 5. E&J Gallo (private, family-owned)

**Top of house.** Joseph Gallo, CEO. Family-owned through trusts; over a dozen fourth-generation family members active in governance. Renamed from "E&J Gallo Winery" to simply "Gallo" in February 2024 to signal cross-category beverage alcohol expansion. **Confirmed.**

**Brand × commercial matrix.** Limited public visibility. Inferred:
- Multi-brand portfolio (Barefoot, Apothic, Gallo Family, Carlo Rossi, plus acquired premium brands like Louis M. Martini, William Hill, Orin Swift, Locations).
- Vertically integrated — 20,000+ planted acres owned, plus extensive grower partnerships.
- **Greg Coleman, VP Grower Relations** — dedicated grower-management function. **Confirmed.**
- **Inferred Chief Commercial Officer** ("B.W." per RocketReach — likely B.W. Williams or similar; not publicly confirmed). **Speculative.**
- ~$5.3B revenue, 7,000 employees, ~20–25% US case volume share.

**Why Gallo matters for GPG.** Gallo is the single closest cultural and structural analog: **family-owned, vertically integrated grower-marketer, multi-brand, multi-channel, private**. They've solved the same problem GPG is solving — how to run a multi-brand commercial function over an owned-supply base with 100+ years of operating history and grower relationships that predate any brand. The dedicated VP Grower Relations role is something GPG should consider explicitly: if the grower interface is run from inside Operations, the commercial team never internalizes supply realities. If it's its own function reporting to CEO or CCO, supply economics become visible to commercial.

**Cautionary tale.** Gallo is private. We can infer org structure but cannot confirm it. The family-governance overlay is something Teays River does NOT have — GPG's PE governance model means its terminal-state structure will look more like a Brown-Forman or Constellation than a Gallo.

## 6. Marriott International (NASDAQ: MAR)

**Top of house.** Tony Capuano, President & CEO. ExCo includes Drew Pinto, President Tina Edmundson (Luxury), and brand-segment Presidents. **Confirmed.**

**The structural answer to "how do you sell 30+ brands to one corporate buyer?"** Marriott's commercial architecture has THREE layers:

1. **Brand Presidents / Chief Brand Officers** own brand equity, brand standards, brand-segment strategy. Tina Edmundson, **President Luxury**, owns Ritz-Carlton, St. Regis, EDITION, Bulgari, Luxury Collection, JW Marriott, W. There are parallel brand-segment Presidents for Premium, Select, and Longer Stays. **Confirmed.**

2. **Drew Pinto, EVP & Chief Revenue & Technology Officer** — runs the **Global Sales Office (GSO)**, revenue management, distribution, and technology under one bundle. In his prior role (Global Officer, Global Sales, Distribution & Revenue Management) he led **6,000+ sales associates** managing **$35B+ revenue and 67M customer contracts annually**. The GSO is the brand-agnostic above-property sales force. **Confirmed.**

3. **GSO field structure** — Account Directors and Sales Executives organized by geography and account portfolio, NOT by brand. A single GSO Account Director owns the Goldman Sachs relationship and pitches Marriott, JW, Westin, Sheraton, Courtyard, Residence Inn etc. as a portfolio for different traveler needs. **"Total Account Management" gives the client a single point of contact across the portfolio.** **Confirmed via Marriott Careers postings and GSO descriptions.**

**Brand pull-through mechanic.** The brand teams set positioning and standards; the GSO sales rep is brand-agnostic but brand-literate. Brand-specific marketing collateral, hotel-level rates, and segment-specific value props get pulled through the GSO conversation. The GSO rep doesn't sell "Marriott" — they sell "the right Marriott property for your traveler segment."

**Vertical integration N/A.** Marriott is asset-light; most hotels are franchised or managed. So commercial-supply coordination in Marriott means commercial-to-owner coordination, not commercial-to-production.

**RGM placement.** Inside Pinto's bundle. Revenue management is one of the four pillars he owns alongside sales, distribution, and technology. **Confirmed.**

**Recent reorgs.** Pinto's role expanded in 2023 to add Technology — a bundled Chief Revenue & Technology Officer seat. This is a parallel to Wayne-Sanderson's bundled CCSO move. **Confirmed.**

**Distinctively GOOD for GPG.** This is the model GPG should study hardest. The GSO + Brand Presidents structure is a working answer to "how do five entities call on Walmart together?" The buyer wants ONE relationship; the brands want THEIR equity protected. Marriott has solved this by:
- Above-property GSO owns the customer relationship.
- Brand Presidents own the brand and influence the GSO conversation.
- A single bundled CRO/CTO seat runs sales + revenue management + distribution + technology as one stack.
- Account-level "total account management" gives the buyer a single point of contact with brand-aware account leads.

**Cautionary tale.** The GSO model requires real account management capability — Account Directors with the strategic chops to orchestrate 30 brands. If GPG tries to do this with traditional produce sales reps, it won't work.

**Brand × commercial matrix.**
- **Chris Silcock, President Global Brands and Commercial Services** — bundled brand-house and commercial-services-house in one C-suite role. Promoted to this role January 2024 after serving as CCO. He oversees the 25-brand portfolio AND brand positioning AND commercial service integration. **Confirmed.**
- **Chris Wilroy, SVP & Chief Commercial Officer** — runs global Revenue Management, Pricing, Sales, Regional Marketing, and Distribution. Reports to Silcock. **Confirmed.**
- **Frank Passanante, SVP Global Head of Sales & HRCC** — leads global sales execution and Hilton Reservations/Customer Care. **Confirmed.**

**Why Hilton matters.** Hilton's Silcock role is the most aggressive brand-and-commercial bundle of any major hospitality company — he runs the equivalent of Marriott's Brand Presidents AND Marriott's Pinto role under one President title. This is a credible model for GPG given that GPG is smaller than Marriott and the case for separating brand-house from commercial-house is weaker at sub-$5B revenue.

**Distinctively GOOD for GPG.** The single bundled "President, Brands and Commercial Services" pattern is structurally cleaner than splitting brand houses from commercial houses for an org GPG's size. One C-suite operator owns "the customer-facing whole."

**Cautionary tale.** This concentration of authority works only if the operator is exceptional. Silcock has 30 years at Hilton. The single-leader model fails fast if the leader is wrong.

## Cross-Industry Patterns

**Pattern 1: The brand-house / commercial-house split is universal in beverages and hospitality, but the bundle level varies.** Diageo and Pernod separate them at the EVP level (Global Brands EVP / Global Markets EVP). Constellation separates them inside each Division (Division President owns both, but reports through CMO and CSO under them). Hilton bundles both under one President (Silcock). Marriott is in between — Brand Presidents AND a CRO, but no single bundle. **The choice of bundle level is mostly about scale.** At GPG's revenue (~$2B inferred), bundling brand and commercial under one CCO is more credible than splitting them.

**Pattern 2: Hospitality's above-property GSO is the closest answer to GPG's Walmart-with-five-entities problem.** Beverages doesn't actually solve this — Diageo's customers are distributors, not retailers, so the brand pull-through happens distributor-by-distributor. Hospitality is the only industry where ONE corporate buyer (e.g., a Goldman Sachs travel manager) buys across 25–30 brands from one company — the same problem as Walmart buying carrots, salads, mash, organic, juices from GPG. **The hospitality answer:** centralize the customer relationship in a brand-agnostic but brand-literate Account Director, organize by account portfolio rather than by brand, and use brand teams to set positioning and feed the conversation. GPG should adopt this pattern explicitly.

**Pattern 3: RGM lives inside commercial, not inside marketing or insights.** Diageo's RGM Managers report through Revenue Growth & Planning, which sits inside commercial. Marriott bundles revenue management inside Pinto's CRO seat. Hilton bundles RGM inside Silcock's President role. The placement signal is consistent across industries: **RGM is a commercial function, not an analytics function**. GPG's Cat Strategy work, if it has an RGM dimension, belongs in the commercial stack — not inside BI.

**Pattern 4: The strategy translation function exists in beverages — it's called Revenue Growth & Planning.** Diageo's Revenue Growth & Planning team is exactly the "translate brand strategy into customer execution" function GPG has been talking about as Cat Strategy. The naming convention varies (RGM, Revenue Growth Planning, Commercial Planning), but the function is universal in scaled multi-brand beverages.

**Pattern 5: Bundled C-suite roles are emerging.** Marriott (Pinto, CRO+CTO), Wayne-Sanderson (Leach, CCSO), Hilton (Silcock, Brands + Commercial), IHG (Balsley, Commercial + Marketing). The trend is **one operator owns more of the customer-facing stack**. The unbundled model (separate CMO, CSO, CCO, CTO, CSCO) is becoming the exception at integrated multi-brand companies.

**Pattern 6: Vertical integration is handled by NOT bundling supply with commercial — except in poultry.** Constellation, Diageo, Brown-Forman, Pernod, Gallo all run supply chain SEPARATELY from commercial. Wayne-Sanderson is the outlier — their CCSO bundle. Why the difference? Poultry has tight bird-cycle supply that has to flex against commercial demand in 6–8 week windows. Beverages has multi-year aging and inventory that decouples supply from commercial timing. **GPG's question:** is fresh produce more like poultry (tight cycle, must-bundle) or like beverages (decoupled, separate)? The answer is closer to poultry — perishable, weekly cycles. The CCSO bundle pattern is more applicable to GPG than the CCO-and-separate-COO pattern.

## What GPG Should Mimic

**1. Adopt the GSO model for key accounts.** The single biggest borrowing opportunity is hospitality's **brand-agnostic, brand-literate, account-portfolio-organized above-property sales force**. For Walmart, Kroger, Costco, and the top 10 retailers, GPG should have ONE National Account Director per top customer — not five, one per entity. That Account Director owns the relationship across carrots, fresh-cut, juice, organic, branded retail. Behind the AD, brand/category teams (carrots, salads, juices, mash, organics) feed positioning, innovation, RGM inputs, and trade programs into the conversation. The AD orchestrates the portfolio sell. **Why it translates:** The buyer wants one relationship. The brands need protection. This pattern has been working in hospitality for 25+ years and is the dominant model in the industry. Confidence: **High** that this is the right structural move.

**2. Build a Revenue Growth & Planning function inside the commercial org, not inside BI or Insights.** Diageo, Pernod, and Marriott all place RGM inside commercial. GPG's Cat Strategy work — if it has an RGM dimension (pricing architecture, pack/channel optimization, promotional ROI) — should sit in the commercial stack reporting to the CCO, not in Jon's BI org. BI should provide the data and analytics; the RGM function should make the calls. **Why it translates:** RGM decisions require commercial accountability — pricing changes hit P&L, and the person making them has to own the result with the customer. Confidence: **High**.

**3. Use a bundled CCSO (Commercial + Supply Chain) as the terminal-state CCO seat.** Wayne-Sanderson's Leach model is more applicable than Constellation's Division President / separate Operations model because GPG has perishable, weekly-cycle supply that has to flex against commercial demand. Bundling supply chain (own farms + merchant) into the commercial seat creates one operator who can trade off supply availability vs. commercial commitments in real time. The pattern across produce peers (Driscoll's bundles supply through grower partnerships, Wonderful runs commercial-and-grower-relations together) supports this. **Why it translates:** Fresh produce supply timing collapses any clean separation between supply and commercial; the integrator role has to be at C-level. Confidence: **High** — already supported by produce-peer research.

**4. Cluster the five entities into 2–3 brand-portfolio groups, not five.** Pernod's Tomorrow 2 clusters 80+ brands into Gold and Crystal divisions. Brown-Forman simplified to Americas / Rest-of-World. The lesson: **don't keep five.** Cluster. For GPG, the natural clusters might be: **Bagged Fresh & Organic** (carrots, organic veg, fresh-cut salads), **Value-Added & Prepared** (mash, juices, prepared meals), and **Foodservice & Industrial**. Each cluster gets a Brand/Category President who owns category P&L; one corporate CCO (the GSO seat) owns customer relationships. **Why it translates:** Five Division Presidents under a CCO is too many direct reports and creates too much internal politics. Three is the manageable number. Confidence: **Medium-high** — the cluster choice itself needs Jon's input.

**5. Create a dedicated Grower Relations function reporting to CCO or CEO.** Gallo's VP Grower Relations is the only public model where this is its own seat — and it's exactly the integration point GPG needs given its mixed merchant + owned supply structure. If grower relations is buried inside Operations, the commercial team never internalizes supply realities; if it's inside the commercial stack, supply economics become visible to customer-facing decision-makers. **Why it translates:** GPG's competitive moat is owned + merchant supply at scale; that moat is operationally complex and needs an explicit organizational owner. Confidence: **Medium-high** — Gallo is the only direct analog and it's private, so we're inferring.

**6. Avoid the brand-house / commercial-house split at GPG's scale.** Diageo and Pernod can afford separate EVP Global Brands and EVP Global Markets seats because they're $20B+ companies. At GPG's scale (~$2B inferred), splitting brand from commercial just creates coordination tax and accountability ambiguity. **The Hilton Silcock model — one President owning Brands AND Commercial Services — is the right size of bundle for GPG.** This argues for a single CCO who owns category brand strategy, customer-facing commercial, and revenue growth management, with the Brand/Category Presidents reporting in. **Why it translates:** GPG isn't big enough to support the overhead of the double-house structure; Hilton at ~$10B revenue is the size where one bundle works. Confidence: **High**.

**Net structural recommendation for the terminal state.** A single CCO/CCSO bundling commercial AND supply chain (Wayne-Sanderson pattern). Underneath: 2–3 Brand/Category Presidents clustering the five legacy entities (Pernod pattern). A National Account / GSO function with brand-agnostic Account Directors for top customers (Marriott pattern). A Revenue Growth & Planning team inside commercial (Diageo pattern). A dedicated Grower Relations seat (Gallo pattern). One CMO (Brown-Forman pattern, post-2025). Supply chain folded into the CCSO seat (poultry pattern — applicable because of perishability).

This would land GPG at a structure that borrows the best of Wayne-Sanderson, Marriott, Hilton, Diageo, Pernod, and Gallo — and avoids the failure modes (Constellation's wine division drift, Diageo's Latin America blowup, Brown-Forman's restructure-during-decline timing) that come from getting any one of those patterns wrong.
