# Shrinkflation in Fresh Produce: Strategic Analysis for GPG

**Date:** April 9, 2026
**Prepared for:** Jon Grove, VP Business Intelligence — Grimmway Produce Group
**Classification:** Internal Use — Strategic Research

---

## Executive Summary

**Shrinkflation is not a viable margin strategy for GPG's core produce categories, particularly baby carrots.** While shrinkflation works effectively in center store (generating roughly half the volume loss of an equivalent price increase), fresh produce operates under fundamentally different structural conditions that neutralize its advantages: weight-based pricing transparency, entrenched pack size standards, weak brand differentiation, and a supplier duopoly that gives retailers easy private label alternatives using the same production base.

The one exception is value-added snack formats (individual portion packs), where format proliferation already creates natural price-per-ounce variation — but this is better framed as price pack architecture optimization than shrinkflation.

**GPG's margin pressure is real, but the answer is mix shift and pricing strategy, not covert downsizing.** This analysis documents why, and identifies five alternative strategies that better fit GPG's structural position.

---

## 1. How Shrinkflation Works in Center Store

### The Core Mechanism

Shrinkflation exploits a well-documented asymmetry in consumer attention: **consumers track price closely but track quantity poorly.** The definitive empirical evidence comes from Janssen & Kasinger (*Marketing Science*, 2025), analyzing a decade of U.S. retail scanner data:

- **Price elasticity: -1.19** — a 1% price increase reduces unit sales by ~1.2%
- **Size elasticity: -0.56** — a 1% size reduction reduces unit sales by only ~0.56%
- **93% of downsized products saw no statistically significant decline in unit sales**
- Downsized products averaged **6% higher unit sales** one year post-reduction

This is why CPG companies keep doing it: the demand destruction is roughly half that of an equivalent price increase. *[Confirmed — peer-reviewed, 10-year scanner data]*

### Scale and Prevalence

- ~**2% of grocery SKUs** experience downsizing in any given year, but these are disproportionately high-volume SKUs
- **33% of grocery items** have been affected at some point (cumulative)
- Peak activity: 2021–2022 during the commodity inflation cycle
- GAO finding: downsized paper towels represented only 3% of SKUs but **38.6% of category dollar sales** — companies shrink their best-sellers, not fringe products
- Shrinkflation contributed only **0.06 percentage points** to aggregate inflation (2019–2024), but category-level effects ranged from **1.6% (cereal) to 32% (coffee)** in per-unit price inflation

*[Confirmed — GAO-25-107451, July 2025]*

### Notable Case Studies

**What worked:**
- **Häagen-Dazs (2009):** 16 oz → 14 oz with transparent communication citing economic conditions. Short-lived backlash, brand recovered. Key factor: no competitor held 16 oz at comparable price.
- **Frito-Lay bonus bag recovery (2024):** After PepsiCo faced backlash over Tostitos/Ruffles downsizing and volume losses to private label, they launched "bonus bags" with 20% more chips. Swift response limited lasting damage.

**What failed:**
- **Toblerone (2016):** Widened gaps between peaks to reduce 170g → 150g. Change was visually undeniable. Massive global backlash, BBC coverage. Reversed in 2018 — but at double the price.
- **Quality Street (ongoing):** 780g → 550g over a decade. Each reduction was individually defensible, but cumulative effect became a meme. M&S launched 1.2kg private label tub as a direct alternative.
- **PepsiCo/Tostitos (2024):** Volume losses to Walmart and Costco private label. The "bonus bags" were a promotional tactic, not a genuine reversal.

**Pattern:** Failures share a common feature — the reduction became visually undeniable, or cumulative reductions became meme-able, or private label offered an obvious alternative at the old size.

## 2. Consumer Behavior and Perception

### Detection and Awareness

Consumer awareness of shrinkflation has surged:
- **82% of Americans** express concern about shrinkflation (Purdue Consumer Food Insights, 2024)
- **75%+ report noticing** within the prior 30 days
- But only **51% regularly check unit price** and **44% check product weight** — detection still relies heavily on secondary channels (social media, news)

*[Confirmed — Purdue/YouGov/Ipsos surveys, 2023–2024]*

### The Critical Asymmetry: Deception vs. Disappointment

The most important finding for GPG's decision: **consumers view shrinkflation as more unfair than equivalent price increases** (Evangelidis, *Marketing Science*, 2024). The mechanism is perceived deception, not economic calculation:

- **79% who notice shrinkflation say they "feel cheated"** (Ipsos, 2023)
- **76% attribute it to profit-seeking**, not cost pressures — a moral judgment, not an economic one
- Consumers intellectually accept price hikes as inflation response, but interpret hidden size reductions as corporate manipulation

The short-term demand advantage (less volume loss) comes with a **disproportionate trust penalty** when discovered. The behavioral cost is deferred, not eliminated.

*[Confirmed — preregistered experimental studies]*

### Social Media Has Changed the Game

Before social media, shrinkflation's "just noticeable difference" strategy worked because detection required individual memory comparison. Social media creates **collective memory:**

- **#shrinkflation exceeded 261 million TikTok views** by mid-2022
- **Reddit r/shrinkflation** has 37,000+ members documenting side-by-side comparisons
- One viral post primes millions of consumers who wouldn't have noticed independently

This effectively **lowers the detection threshold at the population level** even if individual perceptual thresholds remain unchanged. A reduction that would have taken 2–3 repurchase cycles to be noticed can now become nationally discussed within 72 hours.

*[Confirmed — platform metrics; inferred for timeline mechanism]*

### Category Differences: Fresh vs. Packaged

This is directly relevant to GPG:

- Only **18% of consumers** report noticing shrinkflation in fresh produce vs. **68% in snacks** (YouGov/Statista, 2022)
- When produce prices rise, consumers attribute it to **market conditions**; when a chip bag gets lighter, they attribute it to **deception**
- Fresh produce has **less brand switching behavior** even when consumers are annoyed — substitution paths are less clear
- But **detection in produce is structurally easier** due to weight-based pricing — the 18% figure reflects that shrinkflation barely exists in produce, not that it's hard to detect

## 3. The Retailer Perspective

### Retailers Are Participants, Not Victims

The Janssen & Kasinger research revealed that retailers **actively stock downsized products more prominently and for longer** than full-sized predecessors. They benefit from shrinkflation without bearing the consumer backlash, which lands on the manufacturer.

*[Confirmed — Chicago Booth Review analysis of scanner data]*

### But Retailers Also Weaponize It

The dynamic has a second layer:
- **Carrefour (France)** began displaying "shrinkflation notices" on shelves in 2023 — using supplier shrinkflation against them publicly
- **Private label is the structural beneficiary:** U.S. private label sales hit **$282.8 billion in 2025** (record), with 21.3% dollar share. **73% of consumers who switched during inflation say they won't switch back.**
- Retailers can accept brand shrinkflation, let the backlash play out, then capture the switching consumers with private label

### Unit Pricing Creates Shelf Transparency

In **19 states with mandatory unit pricing** (including CA, NY, CT, MA, NJ), shelf tags automatically display price-per-ounce. A 16 oz → 14 oz change at the same price turns a $0.074/oz tag into a $0.085/oz tag — **a 14.3% unit price increase visible on every shelf label.**

States with mandatory unit pricing saw **30–84% greater purchase reduction** for downsized products vs. states without (GAO, tuna fish study).

*[Confirmed — NIST/GAO]*

## 4. Why Shrinkflation Doesn't Work for Fresh Produce

### Structural Barriers

**1. Weight-based pricing transparency.** Produce is sold by weight (loose/bulk) or in bags with net weight prominently displayed. The BLS explicitly categorizes weight-sold products as **structurally exempt from shrinkflation.** Unlike a chip bag where the "unit" is the package, produce units are universally weight-normalized.

**2. Entrenched pack size standards.** Baby carrot pack sizes are deeply embedded:

| Format | Size | Context |
|---|---|---|
| Standard bag | 1 lb (16 oz) | Universal — every major retailer |
| Value bag | 2 lb (32 oz) | Universal |
| Club/bulk | 5 lb (80 oz) | Costco, Sam's Club |
| Snack individual | 2.25–3 oz | Multipack retail, foodservice |

Moving from 16 oz to 14 oz creates a **new SKU**, a **new shelf tag** showing higher price-per-pound, and is immediately visible to every shopper comparing against the 2 lb option.

**3. No brand moat to protect.** In center store, brands have equity that creates switching costs. In produce, brand loyalty is weak — consumers buy baby carrots, not "Grimmway baby carrots." There's no brand relationship to buffer the trust damage.

**4. The duopoly problem.** GPG and Bolthouse collectively supply ~96% of U.S. baby carrots — including much of the private label. If GPG downsizes branded product, retailers can launch 16 oz private label at the old weight **using GPG's own production capacity.** This is the PepsiCo/Tostitos scenario applied to a market where the supplier literally packs both brands.

**5. Visual inspection.** Consumers see, handle, and evaluate fresh produce before buying. A smaller bag is visually obvious. There is no package opacity to exploit.

*[Confirmed for structural analysis; inferred for duopoly competitive dynamics]*

### Categories Where It Could Theoretically Work

The only produce segments with shrinkflation viability are **value-added, brand-differentiated formats:**

| Viability | Category | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Higher | Salad kits | Multi-ingredient, brand-differentiated, consumer focus on kit not weight |
| Higher | Snack packs (individual) | Multiple weights already exist (2.25–3 oz), convenience-driven purchase |
| Moderate | Value-added clamshells | Processing adds brand value, per-package pricing |
| Low | Baby carrots (standard bags) | Commodity, weight-transparent, entrenched sizes |
| Very low | Whole carrots, potatoes, bulk | Weight-priced at retail, not downsizable |

## 5. The Economics: Price Increase vs. Shrinkflation for Baby Carrots

### Margin Math — 16 oz to 14 oz at Constant Price

Applying the Janssen & Kasinger elasticity framework to GPG's situation:

**Assumptions:** $1.19 retail for a 1 lb bag, ~30% gross margin, material cost ~40% of COGS.

| Metric | Price Increase (12.5%) | Shrinkflation (16→14 oz) |
|---|---|---|
| New price | $1.34 | $1.19 (held) |
| COGS per unit | $0.83 (unchanged) | ~$0.80 (material reduction) |
| Margin per unit | $0.51 (+$0.15) | $0.39 (+$0.04) |
| Volume impact (center store elasticity) | -14.9% | -7.0% |
| Total margin (100-unit base) | $43.40 (+$13.40) | $36.27 (+$6.27) |

**In pure math, the price increase produces better total margin** — if volume holds. The reason CPG companies choose shrinkflation is that the volume assumption is more reliable (consumers are less price-sensitive to quantity changes).

**But for produce, the math inverts.** Baby carrots don't have the opacity that makes shrinkflation's volume advantage work. The unit price tag exposes the change. Retailer private label at 16 oz provides an immediate comparison. The volume loss from shrinkflation in produce would likely approach or exceed the volume loss from a transparent price increase — eliminating the advantage while adding the deception penalty.

### The Ratchet Problem

Shrinkflation is a **one-time margin reserve**, not a repeatable strategy:
1. Round 1: Downsize, hold price → margin improves
2. 2–4 years later: Cost pressures return → can't downsize further without viability issues
3. Round 2: Face both price increases AND further downsizing simultaneously — at the worst possible time for consumer trust

Oliver Wyman (2024): "Obvious opportunities to slimline pack sizes have been realized, and products can only shrink so much before they stop being viable."

## 7. Strategic Recommendation

### Should GPG pursue shrinkflation in produce? **No.**

The structural conditions that make shrinkflation effective in center store — package opacity, brand loyalty, consumer inattention to quantity, category complexity — are **absent or inverted** in fresh produce. Baby carrots specifically represent one of the least viable shrinkflation candidates in the entire grocery store.

**The core problem:** GPG supplies ~96% of baby carrots (with Bolthouse). Any branded shrinkflation move hands retailers a straightforward play — launch or expand 16 oz private label **packed by GPG** — that captures the value-conscious consumer while costing GPG the branded volume AND the margin.

### What GPG Should Do Instead

Five alternative strategies that better fit GPG's structural position:

**1. Transparent price increases, anchored to cost narrative.**
The produce category has a natural advantage here: consumers already attribute produce price increases to growing conditions, supply disruptions, and market dynamics — not corporate greed. FMI 2022 data showed produce sales rose 4.8% while volume fell 4.2%, confirming consumers absorbed price increases without catastrophic demand destruction. GPG should lean into this with GroundTruth-style market intelligence that gives retailers the story to tell at shelf.

**2. Mix shift toward higher-margin value-added formats.**
The snack pack / single-serve format is where margin opportunity lives. Multiple weight points already exist (2.25 oz, 2.6 oz, 3 oz). Consumers buy these for convenience, not weight optimization. Expanding the value-added portfolio — organic snack packs, flavored/seasoned formats, meal-prep portions — moves volume into higher-margin segments without touching commodity pack sizes.

**3. Price pack architecture optimization.**
Rather than shrinking existing packs, introduce **new format tiers** that capture different price points. A 12 oz "single-serve household" bag positioned between snack packs and 1 lb bags, at a premium price-per-ounce, is a new SKU — not a downsized old one. This is how Tasteful Selections built the bite-size potato category from 1.5% to 18%+ of the market: new format, new occasion, new price architecture.

**4. Promotional depth reduction.**
Rather than changing pack sizes or base prices, reduce the frequency and depth of promotional pricing. The baby carrot category is heavily promoted. Shallower promotions (e.g., $0.20 off instead of $0.50 off, or 2-for instead of BOGO) protect margin without changing the consumer's reference price for the standard pack. This requires retailer alignment — which is where GroundTruth market briefs become a negotiation tool.

**5. Cost reduction through operational efficiency.**
GPG's vertical integration is a structural advantage that most competitors lack. Margin improvement through yield optimization, processing efficiency, logistics, and waste reduction compound over time without any consumer-facing trade-offs. The current 5-entity integration creates specific opportunities for shared services, procurement consolidation, and capacity utilization that haven't been fully realized.

## 8. Conditions Under Which to Revisit

Shrinkflation could become viable for GPG if:

- **Industry-wide adoption:** If Bolthouse or a major private label partner moves first on produce downsizing, the competitive baseline shifts and the "first mover risk" is eliminated
- **Retail format innovation:** If a new packaging format (e.g., resealable, portion-controlled) creates natural cover for a weight change as part of genuine product innovation
- **Regulatory leveling:** If unit pricing requirements become universal, the transparency gap between shrinkflation and price increases narrows — but this also reduces shrinkflation's effectiveness
- **Cost crisis exceeding price ceiling:** If a genuine supply disruption makes transparent price increases untenable (e.g., retail price points approaching consumer walkaway thresholds), downsizing becomes a last-resort alternative to losing the shelf entirely

None of these conditions appear imminent as of April 2026.

## Data Gaps and Limitations

1. **No peer-reviewed research on shrinkflation in fresh produce specifically.** All academic studies focus on shelf-stable packaged goods. The structural argument against produce shrinkflation is strong but has not been empirically tested.

2. **Elasticity data may not transfer to produce.** The -0.56 size elasticity from Janssen & Kasinger covers center store. Produce — with higher purchase frequency, more visible unit pricing, and less brand loyalty — likely has different elasticity profiles. Internal GPG sales data and Nielsen/Circana category data would be needed to model this with confidence.

3. **Retailer intent is opaque.** No published data on how U.S. produce category managers specifically view pack size changes. Conversations with key retail accounts (Walmart, Kroger, Costco) would provide faster, more actionable intelligence than any public source.

4. **Competitive response from Bolthouse is unpredictable.** If Bolthouse were to downsize and GPG held at 16 oz, GPG could capture a "better value" positioning — but predicting competitor moves from public data isn't possible.

5. **Consumer reaction data is primarily self-reported.** Survey data (79% "feel cheated," 48% "abandoned brands") overstates actual switching behavior. Revealed preference data (private label share growth, CivicScience's 20% acceptance rate) tells a more nuanced story.
