# Organic Vegetables — Slide Brief for Content Creator

## Context

This is for a **single slide** (possibly two) in a presentation being delivered to **college students** by someone other than the author. The audience is not familiar with retail/grocery data. Keep everything simple, visual, and jargon-free. The presenter needs to be able to talk through it without deep category knowledge.

---

## Slide 1: The Big Picture — Organic Produce Is a $22 Billion Category

### Purpose

Set the stage. Show that organic produce has grown massively over the past decade and is now a mature, established category. Frame this positively — the story is "organic arrived," not "growth is slowing."

### Visualization

- **Simple bar chart**, years on x-axis, dollar values on y-axis
- Two visual eras: blue bars (2016-2019 = steady growth years), green bars (2021-2024 = established category)
- 2020 bar in yellow/gold with a small callout label: "COVID Spike"
- Clean, minimal — no gridlines, no decimals needed except where shown
- Large readable labels — this will be projected

### Bullets (on slide, pick 2-3 max)

- Organic produce more than doubled in under a decade — from ~$14B to $22B+
- It's the #1 organic food category, representing ~30% of all organic sales in the U.S.
- COVID accelerated adoption in 2020, and the category has held at that higher level since

### Speaker Notes

> "Organic produce is a $22 billion market in the U.S. and it's the biggest single category in organic food — about a third of all organic sales. What you can see here is that it more than doubled from 2016 to today. There was a big jump in 2020 when COVID pushed consumers toward healthier eating and home cooking. Since then it's held steady at that higher level. So the interesting question isn't really 'is organic growing?' anymore — it's 'where are people buying it?' That's what the next chart gets into."

### Sources

- USDA Economic Research Service — Organic Agriculture
- Supermarket News, The Packer (trade press)
- Note: Produce figures include fruits and vegetables combined. Organic vegetables alone are ~$6B (see Slide 2).

### Visualization Option A — Simple Quadrant (Recommended)

A 2x2 grid. Two axes: Dollar Growth (left = declining, right = growing) and Volume Growth (bottom = declining, top = growing). Zero lines cross in the middle.

- **Upper Right (Winners):** Whole Foods, Walmart, ShopRite, Wegmans, Target, Food Lion, Market Basket
- **Lower Left (Losing Ground):** Kroger, Albertsons, Stop & Shop, Good Food Holdings, Hannaford, Giant

Label the upper-right quadrant "Winners" in green. Label the lower-left "Losing Ground" in red. Keep it clean — no axis numbers needed, just directional.

Optionally: size each retailer's label/dot by their total $ sales so Whole Foods, Walmart, and Kroger appear larger.

### Visualization Option B — Scatter Plot

- X-axis: $ % Change (range: -15% to +10%)
- Y-axis: LBS % Change (range: -15% to +10%)
- Each retailer is a dot, sized by total $ sales
- Color: green for positive $ growth, red for negative
- Label the top 10-12 retailers directly on the chart

### Bullets (on slide, pick 2-3 max)

- Premium (Whole Foods) and value (Walmart, ShopRite) are both growing — the middle is getting squeezed
- Regional grocers like Wegmans and Market Basket are outgrowing the national chains
- Traditional grocery — Kroger, Albertsons, Stop & Shop — is losing organic vegetable shoppers

### Speaker Notes

> "This is organic vegetables specifically — about a $6 billion piece of that $22 billion produce market. The total category is barely growing — less than 1%. But what's interesting is where the growth IS happening. You can see it's concentrating at two ends. Premium retailers like Whole Foods are growing because of the shopping experience and quality positioning. Value retailers like Walmart and ShopRite are growing because they're making organic affordable — Walmart's average price per pound is $2.86, about a dollar cheaper than the category average. Meanwhile, the traditional mid-market grocers — Kroger, Albertsons, Stop & Shop — are all declining. They're stuck in the middle. They can't match Whole Foods on experience and they can't match Walmart on price. The takeaway is: organic shoppers know what they want now. They're either going premium or going value. The middle isn't giving them a reason to stay."

## Design Notes

- **Audience:** College students, not industry professionals. No jargon. No acronyms (spell out everything or skip it).
- **Presenter:** Someone other than the author. Speaker notes should be conversational and self-contained — the presenter should be able to talk through the slide without additional prep.
- **Tone:** Informative, not alarming. This is an intro to the space, not a turnaround story.
- **Color scheme:** Use a consistent palette. Blues/greens for growth, reds/oranges for decline. Keep backgrounds clean.
- **Font sizes:** Large. This will be projected. Body text minimum 18pt, chart labels minimum 14pt.
- **Data density:** Low. One chart per slide maximum. Let whitespace do the work.
- **XAOC:** Stands for "Extended All Outlet Combined" — a syndicated data measurement covering grocery, mass, club, dollar, and military channels. **Do NOT put this acronym on the slides.** If needed, just say "across all major U.S. retailers."
