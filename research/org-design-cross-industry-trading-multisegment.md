# Cross-Industry Org Design — Trading + Owned-Supply Hybrids and Multi-Segment Commercial

**Why this research.** GPG has a structural feature most CPG/produce benchmarks don't address — a merchant-and-owned-supply hybrid commercial reality. RPE potato brokerage runs a merchant book (buy from third-party growers, sell to retailers, manage spread). Tasteful Selections, Grimmway carrots, and Cal-Organic veg run owned-supply allocation (the company grows what it sells). Both businesses sit inside one commercial function. This research goes outside food/ag/CPG to the industries where the trading-vs-production hybrid is the structural norm — global ag merchants, mining/energy traders, and multi-protein meat companies — to find what best-in-class architecture looks like.

**Confidence framing.** Cargill, ADM, Bunge, Tyson, JBS, Sysco are the data-rich anchors (10-Ks, investor days, press releases). Glencore is **partially observable** — segment reporting is public but trading desk internals are notoriously opaque. Pilgrim's is mostly visible because of its public listing. The Wayne-Sanderson case is referenced as known context per `org-design-wayne-sanderson-deep-dive.md` and not re-researched.

## Per-Company Findings

### 1. Cargill — Three-Enterprise Reorg (September 2024)

**Top-of-house architecture.** As of September 2024, Cargill collapsed five enterprises into three: **Food** (Jon Nash), **Ag & Trading** (Roger Watchorn), and **Specialized Portfolio** (David Webster). Each enterprise President reports to CEO Brian Sikes. There is **no Chief Commercial Officer at the corporate level** — commercial sits inside each enterprise. **Confirmed** (Bloomberg, AgWeb, Red River Farm Network, Aug 2024).

**Trading vs. owned-supply split.** This is the most informative element. Cargill folded **Cargill Risk Management and Metals trading** into Ag & Trading alongside the agricultural supply chain (origination, merchandising, storage, freight). The implication: trading is **bundled with origination/supply chain**, not with customer-facing branded sales. Branded protein and food ingredients sit in the separate Food enterprise under Nash. **Confirmed.** This is a deliberate architectural choice: traders sit close to physical assets and origination, while customer-facing sales sits with the branded business.

**Recent reorg.** The 2024 reorg was triggered by FY2024 missing earnings — fewer than a third of businesses hit goals. Cargill cut ~5% of global workforce (~8,000) in December 2024, primarily corporate roles, citing "duplication of work." **Confirmed** (CNN, Star Tribune, Dec 2024).

**Where it goes wrong.** Cargill has restructured five-plus times in two decades. The recurring pattern: when trading margins compress (as in 2023–2024), the "trader-led" cultural posture becomes a liability vs. branded competitors with steadier margins. The 2024 reorg explicitly tries to give the Food enterprise more independence so its ingredient/protein commercial can run on CPG cadence rather than commodity cadence. **Inferred.**

**What's good for GPG.** The cleanest takeaway: **don't bundle trading and branded customer-facing commercial under one leader**. Cargill keeps them in separate enterprises (Ag & Trading vs. Food) precisely because the operating cadences, P&L logic, and customer interfaces are different. For GPG, this argues for keeping RPE merchant activity in its own org node, not folding it generically into a Cal-Organic or Tasteful Selections sales team.

### 2. ADM — Three Segments, Concentration Risk Exposed (2024 Scandal)

**Top-of-house architecture.** ADM organizes into three reporting segments: **Ag Services & Oilseeds** (origination + merchandising + soybean/softseed crush), **Carbohydrate Solutions** (corn/wheat wet & dry milling, starches, sweeteners), and **Nutrition** (specialty ingredients, animal nutrition, flavors). Each segment has a President. **Confirmed** (10-K, ADM IR).

**Trading vs. owned-supply split.** Trading is housed inside **Ag Services & Oilseeds** alongside origination and processing. The "merchandising" function buys from farmers and sells into ADM's own crush plants and to third parties — a classic merchant-plus-processor hybrid. There is no separate "trading desk" P&L exposed externally; it's blended into Ag Services. **Confirmed**.

**The scandal as cautionary tale.** In January 2024, the SEC subpoenaed ADM regarding intersegment accounting practices — specifically how Ag Services was selling raw materials internally to Nutrition at non-market prices, inflating Nutrition's standalone profitability and triggering ~$70M in executive equity bonuses. The internal probe identified a "material weakness" in intersegment accounting; ADM trimmed $228M from Nutrition's operating profit between 2018–2023 and settled with the SEC for $40M in 2026. CFO Vikram Luthar, CEO Juan Luciano, and Nutrition President Vince Macciocchi were all implicated. ADM cut two-thirds of Nutrition brands and 17% of products. **Confirmed** (Fortune, Food Dive, Agriculture Dive, 2024–2026).

**What this teaches GPG.** Internal transfer pricing between an upstream supply business and a downstream branded business is **not an accounting detail — it's a governance issue.** When the upstream business (origination/trading) and the downstream business (branded ingredients) report through the same C-suite officer with bonus exposure to both, transfer pricing distortion is incentivized. For GPG: if RPE potato is buying from third parties and Tasteful Selections is buying its own owned-supply potatoes, the transfer pricing logic between them needs to be **independently governed** — not set by the commercial leader who owns both P&Ls. **Speculative-to-inferred conclusion.**

### 3. Bunge / Viterra — Merger Completed July 2025

**Top-of-house architecture.** Bunge Global completed its $8.2B merger with Viterra on July 2, 2025. CEO Greg Heckman runs the combined company. The architecture is notable: David Mattiske (former Viterra CEO) and Julio Garros (former Bunge Co-President of Agribusiness) sit as **Co-COOs**. There is no single Chief Commercial Officer. **Confirmed** (Bunge press release, Jul 2025).

**Trading vs. owned-supply split.** In October 2025, Bunge announced reporting segment changes effective Q3 2025: four segments — **Soybean Processing & Refining**, **Softseed Processing & Refining**, **Other Oilseeds Processing & Refining**, and **Grain Merchandising & Milling**. The "Grain Merchandising & Milling" segment is the trading + merchant + grain milling function consolidated. Processing is split by commodity type. **Confirmed.**

**This is the new model.** Bunge has separated **trading/merchandising from processing** at the segment level. Trading lives in one segment with a single P&L line, processing is broken out by commodity. Processing P&Ls are about asset utilization and crush margin; the merchandising segment is about origination, freight, basis trading, and the merchant book. **Confirmed.**

**What's good for GPG.** Bunge's post-merger architecture is the cleanest expression of "separate the merchant book from the asset/branded P&Ls." If GPG were to mimic this, RPE's merchant brokerage would be **its own segment-equivalent P&L line** (basis-trading-style margin) reporting up the same CEO/CCO as the owned-supply branded segments — but not commingled with them.

### 4. Glencore — The Industrial-vs-Marketing Split (the Reference Pattern)

**Top-of-house architecture.** Glencore is the cleanest publicly-traded model of the trading-vs-production hybrid. CEO Gary Nagle runs the company. Two reporting segments: **Industrial** (owned mines, smelters, oil assets) and **Marketing** (the global trading desk — 1,500+ traders across 50+ offices). Both segments report to the CEO. There is no CCO above them; they are **structural peers**. **Confirmed** (Glencore 2024 Preliminary Results, Mining Weekly).

**Trading vs. production split — the gold standard.** In 2024, Industrial generated $10.6B Adjusted EBITDA (down 20% YoY due to commodity prices); Marketing generated $3.2B Adjusted EBIT — at the upper end of guidance. After divesting Viterra (sold to Bunge), Glencore raised long-term Marketing Adjusted EBIT guidance to $2.3–3.5B annually with a $2.9B midpoint. **Confirmed.**

**The structural separation matters because:**
- **P&Ls run on different math.** Industrial is asset-utilization economics. Marketing is spread/basis trading economics.
- **Customer interfaces differ.** Industrial sells offtake at production cost-plus. Marketing trades third-party physical and Glencore's own production fungibly into the global book.
- **Risk management differs.** Industrial hedges production cost; Marketing manages position risk daily.
- **Compensation differs.** Marketing traders are bonused on book P&L. Industrial managers are bonused on operational metrics.

**The bridge: vertical synergy without commingling.** Glencore's edge is that **Marketing has first call on Industrial production** — but at market-clearing prices, with the trading desk obligated to sell that production into the most profitable channel globally. The two organizations are connected by an **internal commercial agreement**, not by being the same team. **Confirmed pattern** (Glencore investor materials).

**Where it goes wrong.** Glencore's trading desk is famously opaque — known unknowns include exact trader compensation and book-level risk. The 2018–2022 period saw multiple trading-desk-related compliance/legal exposures (DOJ corruption cases, sanctions probes). The lesson: **separating Marketing structurally does not exempt it from corporate governance** — but it does mean compliance, risk, and ethics must be embedded **inside Marketing**, not delegated to a corporate function. **Confirmed.**

**What's good for GPG.** The two-segment Industrial vs. Marketing model is **directly applicable to GPG's RPE problem.** If GPG framed the commercial function as (a) Owned-Supply Allocation (TS, Grimmway carrots, Cal-Organic veg) and (b) Merchant/Trading Book (RPE potato brokerage), with both reporting to the CCO and connected by an internal commercial agreement, that's the Glencore architecture transplanted to produce. **Strongly inferred.**

### 5. Tyson Foods — Multi-Segment Commercial with a Chief Customer Officer

**Top-of-house architecture.** Tyson reports five segments: **Chicken (Poultry)**, **Beef**, **Pork**, **Prepared Foods**, **International**. Each has a Group President. As of Sept 2025, Devin Cole is COO overseeing all segments; Brady Stewart is Group President of Beef, Pork, and Prepared Foods AND Chief Supply Chain Officer (a bundled role similar to Wayne-Sanderson's CCSO). **Confirmed** (Tyson IR, Sept 2025).

**Customer × category coverage.** Tyson uses a **hybrid** model:
- **Chief Customer Officer (Jason Nichol)** runs an enterprise-wide team of 850+ professionals serving major customers across all proteins — national restaurant chains, grocery, broadline distributors, schools, clubs, dollar, c-store, non-commercial. Nichol came from the Hillshire Walmart team; the design is one-Tyson-team-per-major-customer covering chicken+beef+pork+prepared. **Confirmed.**
- **Segment Group Presidents** own product strategy, brand, R&D, and category-level commercial within each protein.

This is the **centralized key accounts + segment-led brand teams** hybrid model. It solves the "don't show up at Walmart with three competing reps" problem.

**Chief Growth Officer (Kristina Lambert, May 2025).** Replaced Melanie Boulden. The CGO role at Tyson owns enterprise growth strategy, marketing, innovation, and category/brand investment — sitting alongside the CCO (Nichol) rather than above. Two CXOs running commercial is unusual but reflects Tyson's scale. **Confirmed.**

**Vertical integration and commercial bundling.** Tyson **does** bundle supply chain with one segment leader (Stewart's Beef/Pork/Prepared + CSCO). But customer-facing commercial (Nichol) is its own line. So Tyson has separated "supply chain + segment ops" from "customer-facing sales," which is the inverse of the Wayne-Sanderson 2026 CCSO bundling. **Confirmed.**

**What's good for GPG.** The Tyson Chief Customer Officer model — one team per major customer covering all categories — is **directly mimicable for GPG's Walmart, Kroger, Costco, Sysco, US Foods relationships**, where multiple GPG entities currently call separately. A "GPG Customer Officer" running cross-category teams at top-25 customers is a clean architectural answer.

### 6. JBS USA / Pilgrim's Pride — Segment-Federated Commercial

**Top-of-house architecture.** JBS S.A. operates seven reportable segments globally: Brazil, Seara, **Beef North America**, **Pork USA**, **Pilgrim's Pride** (separately listed with JBS owning 82%), Australia, and Other. Each segment has its own commercial leadership; there is **no JBS-USA-wide Chief Commercial Officer.** **Confirmed.**

**Pilgrim's commercial structure.** CEO Fabio Sandri runs a segment-led commercial structure organized by **bird type and channel**: **Big Bird** (foodservice/QSR), **Small Bird** (QSR), **Case Ready** (retail), and **Prepared Foods** (Just Bare brand, $1B+ retail). Each portfolio segment has different sales motions — Big Bird sells to commodity foodservice on weekly contracts; Case Ready sells to retail on category management cadence; Prepared Foods runs CPG-style brand-led commercial. **Confirmed** (Pilgrim's IR, MEAT+POULTRY).

**Federated commercial.** JBS does NOT centralize commercial across segments. Pilgrim's runs its own commercial; Beef NA runs its own; Pork USA runs its own. Major retailers like Walmart see **multiple JBS teams** — one from each segment. JBS's stated position is that protein-specific expertise outweighs the cost of multi-touch customer coverage. **Confirmed.**

**Where it goes wrong.** JBS has been criticized in trade press for retailer fatigue — buyers describing "five separate JBS conversations." JBS has invested in joint business planning processes to coordinate, but the structural choice is federation, not integration. **Inferred.**

**What's good for GPG.** Pilgrim's bird-type / channel commercial structure is informative for GPG's owned-supply businesses: **commercial structure should follow product cadence**, not just product type. RPE merchant brokerage runs a different cadence than Tasteful Selections retail commercial — they need separate commercial leaders even if they share a CCO.

### 7. Sysco — Closest Structural Analog to GPG (Distribution + Brand + Specialty)

**Top-of-house architecture.** Sysco is the cleanest structural analog to GPG's mixed commercial reality. They distribute thousands of SKUs from hundreds of suppliers (broadline merchant/distributor function), run a $9B+ Specialty business (FreshPoint, Buckhead/Newport Meat, Greco & Sons Italian, Edward Don, Asian specialty), and run **Sysco Brand private label** as a competing branded portfolio. **Confirmed** (Sysco 2025 Annual Report).

**Customer-facing organization.** Sysco's sales force is structured as:
- **Marketing Associates (MAs)** — 7,000+ commission/consultative sales reps assigned to customer territories within District operating companies. They sell the full Sysco book — broadline + specialty + Sysco Brand. **Confirmed.**
- **District Sales Managers** — coach MAs, manage territory plans, prioritize independent restaurant relationships.
- **Corporate Multi-Unit (CMU)** — separate national accounts team for chain restaurant customers (Cheesecake Factory, Wendy's, etc.) with corporate-level contracts.
- **Specialty companies** — FreshPoint, Newport Meat, Greco & Sons run as **separate operating companies with their own sales forces**, but cross-sold via the broadline MA network.

**Cross-sell economics.** When a broadline customer adds FreshPoint produce, revenue from that customer increases 3x; adding specialty meat (Buckhead/Newport) makes it 6x. **Confirmed** (Sysco Investor Day 2024). This is the structural argument for keeping specialty companies operationally separate but commercially networked.

**Sysco Brand placement.** Sysco Brand private label is procurement-and-marketing-led, not sold by a separate sales force. MAs sell Sysco Brand alongside national brands; the MA is incentivized to convert customers to Sysco Brand because it carries higher gross margin. **Confirmed** (Sysco 10-K).

**Spot/contract split.** Sysco runs a **product procurement function** centrally — buying from suppliers on contract terms. Spot/merchant activity (proteins, produce sourcing) sits inside the specialty companies (FreshPoint for produce, Newport for protein). This is the most informative finding: **Sysco separates broadline contract-supply from specialty merchant-supply at the operating-company level**, while integrating both at the customer-facing MA level. **Confirmed.**

**What's good for GPG.** This is the most copyable model. Sysco's "**federated supply, integrated customer**" architecture maps directly to GPG: keep RPE merchant operations as a separate operating unit (like FreshPoint inside Sysco), keep Tasteful/Grimmway/Cal-Organic as their own branded supply units, but unify the customer-facing layer (MA equivalent — one GPG sales rep at each key customer covering everything).

## Trading + Owned-Supply Patterns (Best-in-Class Architecture)

Across Cargill, ADM, Bunge/Viterra, and Glencore, three architectural patterns emerge. The differences are diagnostic — they tell you when each works.

### Pattern A — Marketing/Trading as a Standalone Segment Peer (Glencore Model)

**Structure.** Trading desk runs as its own reportable P&L peer to Industrial/Asset segments. CEO sits above both. Internal commercial agreement governs the price at which Industrial sells production into Marketing.

**When it works.** When the trading book is large enough to be margin-relevant (Glencore Marketing = $2.5–3.5B annual EBIT; Cargill's trading book is similar magnitude). When the trading function has a global third-party physical book, not just selling owned production. When commodity price volatility is structural and traders earn alpha worth segregating.

**When it breaks.** When the trading book is too small to justify its own infrastructure (compliance, risk, comp). When traders' value-add is mostly internal optimization (selling owned production efficiently) rather than third-party basis trading.

**Confidence: Confirmed pattern.**

### Pattern B — Trading Embedded in Origination/Supply Chain (Cargill Ag & Trading, ADM Ag Services & Oilseeds)

**Structure.** Trading function is inside the origination/supply chain segment, alongside merchandising, freight, and storage. Branded/processed business is a separate segment. Trading P&L is not externally visible — embedded.

**When it works.** When traders' primary role is supporting origination and asset throughput (sourcing inputs for owned crush plants, optimizing freight basis). When the trading function is structurally captive — most volume is internal.

**When it breaks.** Internal transfer pricing becomes the governance flashpoint (ADM Nutrition scandal). When upstream and downstream P&Ls share executives, intersegment pricing manipulation becomes possible and is hard to detect.

**Confidence: Confirmed pattern with documented failure mode.**

### Pattern C — Federated Commercial by Product/Segment (JBS, pre-merger Bunge)

**Structure.** Each protein/commodity segment runs its own commercial including any merchant/trading activity. No corporate-level integration of customer-facing commercial.

**When it works.** When products are commercially unrelated (beef and chicken share little customer overlap at the buying decision level) or when segment-specific expertise is the key alpha.

**When it breaks.** At major retailers/distributors who buy across segments and demand integrated coverage. JBS's retailer-fatigue criticism is the canonical complaint.

**Confidence: Confirmed pattern with documented friction.**

### The Common Architectural Element

In all three patterns, the **trading desk and the branded customer-facing sales force are structurally separate organizations** — even when they ultimately roll up to the same CEO. Nobody integrates traders and branded sellers in one team. The reason: cadence, P&L logic, customer interface, risk profile, and compensation are all different.

The integration mechanism is always one of two things:
1. **An internal commercial agreement** governing how owned production is priced into the trading book (Glencore, Bunge post-merger).
2. **A central account team** that calls on the customer with a unified offer (Tyson Chief Customer Officer, Sysco MA).

Companies use one or the other — rarely both.

## What GPG Should Mimic for the RPE Problem

GPG's specific structural problem: **RPE is a potato merchant brokerage running a trading/spread book, sitting inside a commercial function that also handles owned-supply allocation for Tasteful Selections, Grimmway carrots, and Cal-Organic veg.** The merchant cadence (basis, spread, third-party supply) is fundamentally different from the allocation cadence (owned production planning, branded customer commitment).

Three concrete structural recommendations, drawn from the cross-industry patterns above.

### Recommendation 1 — Separate the Merchant P&L from the Owned-Supply P&L (Glencore Pattern)

Run RPE potato merchant brokerage as its own reportable P&L line under the CCO, structurally peer to Tasteful Selections, Grimmway, and Cal-Organic — not folded into one of them. RPE's economics are basis-trading and spread-management, not gross-margin-on-owned-production. Commingling them obscures both. **The key architectural choice: RPE has its own commercial leader who reports to the CCO, with no operational reporting line into Tasteful Selections leadership** — even though both touch potato.

This is the Glencore Marketing-vs-Industrial pattern transplanted. Both segments report to the same CEO/CCO; neither is subordinate to the other.

### Recommendation 2 — Govern Internal Transfer Pricing Outside the Commercial Line (ADM Cautionary Tale)

If RPE ever buys from or sells to Tasteful Selections (e.g., RPE sources potatoes that Tasteful packs, or Tasteful overflow sells through RPE merchant channels), the **transfer price between them must not be set by the commercial leader**. The ADM Nutrition scandal shows what happens when a single executive owns both the upstream selling P&L and the downstream buying P&L — bonus exposure incentivizes manipulation.

Practical implementation: a Finance/Controlling-led transfer pricing committee with an independent benchmark (USDA/Idaho potato basis, e.g.) governing internal pricing. The CCO consumes the result; doesn't set it. This is governance, not commercial — and it should be visible in the audit committee charter.

### Recommendation 3 — Unify the Customer-Facing Layer with a Tyson/Sysco Model

The merchant/allocation distinction matters for **how product is supplied**. It does NOT need to matter for **how the customer is covered**. Walmart does not care whether GPG's potatoes came from RPE merchant supply or Tasteful owned supply — they want one buyer relationship.

Mimic the Tyson Chief Customer Officer model: a unified customer-facing team for top-25 GPG customers that covers all categories (potatoes, carrots, organic veg) and all sources (merchant + owned). The team has commitments from each supply unit (RPE, TS, Grimmway, Cal-Organic) but speaks with one voice to the buyer. The Sysco MA model is the same idea at the field-rep level: one GPG rep covers everything for that customer.

This separates **structural P&L architecture** (federated supply, separate trading book) from **customer architecture** (integrated key accounts). That's the pattern that wins.

### Bonus — The Wayne-Sanderson Pattern Doesn't Quite Apply Here

Wayne-Sanderson's April 2026 reorg bundled Commercial AND Supply Chain into one CCSO seat (Aaron Leach). That bundling works for them because Wayne-Sanderson is a single-protein, fully-owned-supply operation — there is no third-party merchant book, so the supply function is purely about owned-production allocation. **GPG's RPE situation is materially different.** Bundling supply chain and commercial under one officer at GPG would force a single executive to govern both the merchant trading book and the owned-supply allocation — exactly the configuration that ADM's Nutrition scandal illustrated as governance-fragile.

The Wayne-Sanderson pattern is relevant for the four owned-supply businesses. It's the wrong pattern for the RPE merchant book.

## Multi-Segment Commercial — What Tyson, JBS, and Pilgrim's Teach

GPG also runs a multi-category commercial reality (potatoes, carrots, organic veg, plus emerging adjacencies). Three patterns from the protein companies on multi-segment commercial.

### The Two Endpoints

**Tyson endpoint — Centralized customer, segmented brand.** Chief Customer Officer (Nichol) owns the customer relationship across all proteins; segment Group Presidents own brand, P&L, and category. One team at Walmart for chicken+beef+pork+prepared. The risk is that the CCO function becomes a coordination overhead with limited authority over segment P&Ls.

**JBS endpoint — Federated by segment.** Each segment runs its own commercial fully. Pilgrim's calls on Walmart separately from Beef NA from Pork USA. Maximizes segment-specific expertise; minimizes coordination overhead; creates customer-facing fragmentation.

The actual choice depends on how much your customer base values cross-category integration. For grocery retail buying produce, the answer is high — Walmart has one produce buyer for potatoes, carrots, and bagged salads. For protein, the answer is split — chicken and beef are separate buyer organizations at most retailers.

### Pilgrim's Internal Pattern — Channel-Defined Commercial

Pilgrim's organizes its commercial by channel (Big Bird / Small Bird / Case Ready / Prepared Foods), not by customer or by region. Each channel has different cadence: Big Bird is foodservice contract-driven (weekly), Case Ready is retail category-driven (quarterly review cycles), Prepared Foods is CPG brand-driven. Different commercial leaders for different cadences, even within one protein. **This is directly applicable to GPG** — the cadence of selling to Sysco is different from the cadence of selling to Costco is different from the cadence of running RPE's spot foodservice book. Channel-defined sub-leaders inside the commercial function is a clean way to handle this.

### Vertical Integration + Commercial Bundling

Tyson bundles **Supply Chain with Beef/Pork/Prepared** segments under Brady Stewart. Wayne-Sanderson bundles **Commercial + Supply Chain** under Aaron Leach. Cargill bundles **Trading + Origination + Supply Chain** under Watchorn. The pattern: when a function is upstream and asset-heavy, it's bundled with operations. When it's downstream and customer-facing, it's bundled with commercial. **The CCSO bundle is rare and specific to single-channel, fully-integrated companies.** GPG's mixed-channel, mixed-supply reality probably argues for separate CCO and CSCO seats, with coordination via the executive team — not bundling.

### The Recommendation

For GPG's multi-category commercial: build the Tyson Chief Customer Officer + Pilgrim's channel-defined commercial leaders inside one CCO function. The CCO owns customer architecture (one GPG team at Walmart, Kroger, Costco, Sysco). Beneath the CCO, channel-defined commercial leaders own cadence-specific commercial (retail category management, foodservice, merchant/trading, etc.). Brand/innovation sits with each owned-supply business unit. Supply chain sits separately under a CSCO.
