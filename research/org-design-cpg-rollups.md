# Commercial Org Design at CPG Rollup Companies

How five public CPG rollups have structured the Sales / Marketing / Category Management / Trade Marketing matrix as they absorbed multiple brands or entities. The companies sit on a spectrum: from fully federated holding-company (Post) to fully centralized customer-facing (Conagra), with B&G, TreeHouse, and Utz operating hybrid models that have visibly oscillated.

## Executive Summary

- **Sales is almost always centralized.** Four of five companies run a single corporate sales / Customer Leadership Organization that brings the full portfolio to retailers. Post is the explicit exception — it operates as a true holding company with separate sales orgs per operating company.
- **Marketing federates with the brand portfolio.** Brand teams sit inside business segments / divisions, not at corp. None of the five has a strong "Chief Marketing Officer of the whole portfolio" role; brand-building lives under segment presidents.
- **The Chief Commercial Officer / Chief Customer Officer role is unstable.** Three of five (B&G, TreeHouse, Utz) added a CCO during integration phases and then either eliminated it (TreeHouse 2025), repurposed it into segment-president roles (B&G 2025), or evolved it into a Chief Customer Officer scope (Utz). The CCO is integration scaffolding, not a permanent fixture.
- **Trade Marketing / RGM sits inside Sales** at all five — never as a separate function reporting to the CFO or CEO directly.
- **Steady-state archetype** for a multi-brand, multi-category but single-channel CPG: one CCO over a single customer-facing sales force; segment presidents owning P&L, brand marketing, and innovation; RGM and shopper insights embedded in the customer org.

## Conagra Brands

The cleanest example of a centralized commercial model post-rollup. Confirmed via current corporate leadership page and 10-K segment reporting.

**1. Commercial scope.** Conagra splits commercial leadership in two. Derek De La Mater is **EVP and Chief Customer Officer** — leads the "Customer Leadership Organization" which is Conagra's name for the single sales force. Tom McGough is **EVP and Chief Operating Officer** with oversight of all four reporting segments (Refrigerated & Frozen, Grocery & Snacks, Foodservice, International), R&D, and Precision Marketing. There is no corporate CMO and no corporate Chief Commercial Officer — that scope is split between De La Mater (customer/sales) and McGough (segments/operations/marketing). *Confirmed.*

**2. Entity/brand × function matrix.** Hybrid leaning centralized:
- Sales: **fully centralized** at corp under De La Mater. One Customer Leadership Organization sells the entire portfolio (Birds Eye, Hunt's, Marie Callender's, Slim Jim, Duncan Hines, Wish-Bone, Healthy Choice, Reddi-wip, etc.) to "grocery chains, broadline distributors, membership clubs, dollar and drug stores, c-stores and non-traditional outlets." *Confirmed from CCO bio.*
- Marketing: **federated to segment.** Brand teams live inside segments under segment presidents (Noelle O'Mara runs Refrigerated & Frozen as EVP and President; Burke Raine runs New Platforms / Acquisitions / Growth Excellence). McGough as COO sits above the segment presidents. *Confirmed.*
- Innovation / Precision Marketing / Demand Science: **centralized** under McGough (and historically under Co-COO Darren Serrao). *Confirmed.*

**3. Customer × category matrix.** Single key-account team carrying the full portfolio. Customer Team Leaders (e.g., Walmart, Kroger, Costco, Albertsons leads) are part of the Customer Leadership Organization and pull in segment-level brand and shopper resources for category line reviews. *Inferred from "Customer Leadership Organization" framing and standard practice.*

**4. Trade Marketing / RGM.** Conagra has invested heavily in RGM (digital pricing/promo, pack architecture). Job postings ("Revenue Growth Manager, Global Markets") sit in Sales. RGM is a centralized analytics/strategy capability that operates inside or adjacent to the Customer Leadership Organization, not a separate function reporting to the CFO. *Confirmed (postings) / Inferred (reporting line).*

**5. Recent reorgs.** Pinnacle integration (2018-2021) is the model rollup case. Conagra eliminated Pinnacle's standalone commercial structure (Mark Schiller had been Pinnacle's EVP, CCO & Interim CCO) and absorbed all Pinnacle brands into the existing Conagra segment + Customer Leadership Organization model. Conagra also took a "diagnostic" approach to clean up Pinnacle's underperforming brands rather than running them as a separate division. The Co-COO structure (McGough + Serrao) operated 2020-2024 and has since simplified. *Confirmed.*

**6. Multi-segment P&L.** Segments have presidents (O'Mara for R&F, Raine for New Platforms) who own P&L and roll up to a corporate COO — they do **not** have their own commercial leaders. Sales is shared across segments. *Confirmed.*

## B&G Foods

The most active recent example of going **from** functional commercial structure **to** segment-presidential. The August 2024 reorg is directly relevant to GPG.

**1. Commercial scope.** Until August 2024, B&G ran a centralized model: Jordan Greenberg as **EVP & Chief Commercial Officer**, Ellen Schum as **EVP & Chief Customer Officer**, and Vanessa Maskal as **EVP Sales & Marketing**. Effective August 2024, CEO Casey Keller reorganized into **four business units** (Spices & Seasonings 18%, Meals 22%, Frozen & Vegetables 27%, Specialty 33%), each with its own president. Greenberg became President of Spices & Seasonings; Schum became President of Specialty; Kristen Thompson (formerly marketing director for Green Giant) was promoted to President of Frozen & Vegetables. *Confirmed.*

**2. Entity/brand × function matrix.** Moved from centralized commercial-functional to **federated business-unit** structure. Each BU president now owns their own brands (e.g., Crisco, Ortega, Cream of Wheat, Green Giant, Maple Grove Farms, Las Palmas) and presumably the supporting commercial functions for that BU. Sales force allocation post-reorg is unclear from public sources — possible the customer-facing sales team remains centralized while marketing and category management federate to BUs. *Inferred / Speculative on sales force allocation.*

**3. Customer × category matrix.** Pre-2024 reorg: a single B&G sales force called on all customers with the full ~50 brand portfolio. Post-reorg: still likely one customer-facing team, but BU presidents now own resource allocation. *Inferred.*

**4. Trade Marketing / RGM.** Historically embedded in Sales under the CCO (Greenberg). Post-reorg likely splits across BU presidents with central coordination. *Inferred.*

**5. Recent reorgs.** August 2024 reorg from functional-commercial to BU-presidential is the headline. Keller's framing: "establish clear focus and expectations… defining the categories and brands that will be resourced and grown, platforms for future acquisitions, and brands that may be exited over time." Reads as a portfolio rationalization tool dressed as an org structure. *Confirmed.*

**6. Multi-segment P&L.** New structure gives each BU president full P&L. Notable: B&G operates a "headquarters-light" capital model (~10 owned plants + heavy co-packer network), so the BU presidents have less operational asset base than typical segment presidents elsewhere — they're closer to brand-portfolio P&L owners than full operating-company GMs. *Confirmed (capital model) / Inferred (BU scope).*

## Post Holdings

The federated extreme — explicit holding-company model. Bill Stiritz built the philosophy: decentralization is "the ultimate" structure; segment managers run autonomous operating companies.

**1. Commercial scope.** **No corporate CCO, CMO, or CSO.** Post Holdings corporate is a small capital-allocation HQ (Rob Vitale as CEO). Each operating company has its own commercial leadership: Post Consumer Brands (cereal + pet food + peanut butter), Weetabix (UK cereal), Michael Foods (foodservice eggs/potatoes), Bob Evans Farms / Refrigerated Retail (refrigerated sides/sausage), 8th Avenue Food & Provisions (private brands JV with Thomas H. Lee). *Confirmed.*

**2. Entity/brand × function matrix.** **Fully federated.** Each operating company has its own complete commercial organization:
- **Post Consumer Brands**: Greg Pearson incoming President & CEO (April 2026), succeeding Nicolas Catoggio (now Post Holdings COO). Own CMO (Claudine Patel), own sales force, own customer teams. Has integrated Pet Food (acquired April 2023 from Smucker for $1.2B — Rachael Ray Nutrish, Nature's Recipe, 9Lives, Kibbles 'n Bits, Gravy Train) into a "new pet food platform within Post Consumer Brands" rather than as a separate Holdings segment. *Confirmed.*
- **Michael Foods**: Mark Westphal as President & CEO. Sales aligned by **distribution channel** (foodservice, retail, food ingredient) — not by brand or category. *Confirmed.*
- **Refrigerated Retail (Bob Evans)**: Mike McCoy as President & CEO since October 2024. *Confirmed.*
- **Weetabix**: Dedicated UK leadership; recently shuffled with O'Dwyer's promotion and Commercial Director vacancy backfill. *Confirmed.*

**3. Customer × category matrix.** Each OpCo calls on customers separately. Walmart sees a Post Consumer Brands team for cereal and pet food; a different Michael Foods team for foodservice eggs; a different Bob Evans team for refrigerated sausage and sides. **No unified Post Holdings key-account model.** *Inferred from OpCo separation.*

**4. Trade Marketing / RGM.** Lives inside each OpCo's commercial organization. *Inferred.*

**5. Recent reorgs.** The interesting move is **inside** Post Consumer Brands — Catoggio integrated Pet Food onto the existing PCB platform (sales force, customer teams, marketing) rather than running it as its own holding-co segment. This is the only place Post has *centralized* anything across acquired entities, and it's at the OpCo level, not at Holdings. Catoggio's promotion to Post Holdings COO (January 2026) may suggest Holdings is starting to add a thin commercial-coordination layer, but no evidence yet of cross-OpCo sales integration. *Confirmed (Pet Food integration into PCB) / Speculative (Holdings COO implications).*

**6. Multi-segment P&L.** Each segment president runs a full operating company with own commercial, supply chain, finance. Holdings provides M&A, capital, treasury. This is the textbook PE-style federated model. *Confirmed.*

## TreeHouse Foods

The cautionary tale on commercial structure instability. Has reorganized at least three times in five years.

**1. Commercial scope.** Currently in flux. Steve Oakland (CEO since 2018, Chair since 2023) sits atop a structure that just (May 2025) **eliminated the Chief Commercial Officer role**. Scott Tassani had been EVP, Business President & Chief Commercial Officer; he stepped down May 30, 2025, and the position was eliminated as part of a 150-job cut centralizing corporate support functions. *Confirmed.*

**2. Entity/brand × function matrix.** The original 2020-2021 structure: two divisions (Snacking & Beverages; Meal Preparation), each with a division president, plus a CCO over both. Kevin Jackson held the unusual dual role of President Snacking & Beverages **and** Interim/then-permanent CCO, with Sean Lewis as SVP & Chief Customer Officer reporting to Jackson — i.e., a CCO over the divisions plus a CCO under him for the customer-facing sales force. After 2025 elimination of the CCO role, the divisional presidents likely report directly to Oakland, and the customer org sits below them. *Confirmed (history) / Inferred (current state).*

**3. Customer × category matrix.** TreeHouse is private label, so its customer model is unusual: customers (Walmart, Kroger, Costco) are also the brand owners. Customer Team Leaders work to win bid awards across many categories. The 2020 division reorg was explicitly framed as "organized according to market dynamics," moving from product-category divisions to channel/dynamics-based ones. *Confirmed.*

**4. Trade Marketing / RGM.** Less applicable than for branded companies — private label trade economics are different. Pricing/RGM-equivalent functions sit with Customer Team Leaders and Finance. *Inferred.*

**5. Recent reorgs.** Multiple. (a) 2020: 3 divisions → 2 divisions (Snacking & Beverages; Meal Preparation). (b) 2021: appointed permanent CCO. (c) 2024: 150 corporate role cuts, "operational enhancement actions." (d) 2025: eliminated CCO role, further centralization of corporate functions. (e) February 2026: Investindustrial took TreeHouse private for $2.9B — additional restructuring likely under PE ownership. *Confirmed.*

**6. Multi-segment P&L.** Division presidents own P&L for Snacking & Beverages vs Meal Preparation; sales force is largely shared given private-label customer concentration. *Inferred.*

## Utz Brands

The most recent rollup case (post-2020 SPAC) and the most interesting Chief Customer Officer evolution.

**1. Commercial scope.** Mark Schreiber as **EVP of Sales and Chief Customer Officer** — joined as CCO in 2017, promoted in 2021 to also run all sales (DSD + warehouse), trade marketing/revenue management, and category/shopper insights. Effectively the corporate commercial leader. Reports to CEO (Dylan Lissette historically; Howard Friedman as of 2022). No separate CCO/CMO at corporate. *Confirmed.*

**2. Entity/brand × function matrix.** **Centralized** — single sales organization across all brands.
- Sales: one Utz Brands sales org that covers Utz, On The Border, Zapp's, Boulder Canyon, Golden Flake, Vitner's, etc. Schreiber explicitly "took on added responsibility for the about $300 million of retail sales… driven through Utz's Truco and On the Border Division" — i.e., Truco was an acquired brand that started as a separate division, then folded into the unified sales org. *Confirmed.*
- Marketing: brand teams centralized at corp under "Power Brands vs Foundation Brands" framework. Utz, On The Border, Boulder Canyon, Zapp's are the four "Power Brands" getting marketing investment; Foundation Brands are de-emphasized. *Confirmed.*
- Distribution: hybrid DSD (legacy DSD markets) + warehouse direct. Recent (2024-2025) shift toward Independent Operator (IO) route conversions — converting employee DSD routes to franchisee model. *Confirmed.*

**3. Customer × category matrix.** Single key-account team carries the full Power Brands portfolio. ~125,000 retail outlets weekly. *Confirmed.*

**4. Trade Marketing / RGM.** Sits **inside** Schreiber's CCO scope — explicitly: "managing trade marketing/revenue management, and category/shopper insights." This is the cleanest example of the embedded-RGM-in-Sales model. *Confirmed.*

**5. Recent reorgs.** Two parallel transformations 2020-2026: (a) **portfolio rationalization** — sold Good Health and R.W. Garcia brands and two manufacturing facilities ($182.5M); reduced manufacturing footprint from 16 to 7 plants; (b) **commercial focus** — Power Brands vs Foundation Brands segmentation, "Expansion Geographies" growth pillar (West Coast / Sun Belt push), IO route conversions. The CAGNY 2026 narrative emphasizes that supply chain transformation is "largely complete." *Confirmed.*

**6. Multi-segment P&L.** Utz reports as a single segment — no segment presidents. Truco / On The Border was briefly a "Division" but has been absorbed. *Confirmed.*

## Cross-Company Patterns

**Two clean archetypes plus a hybrid spectrum.** Of these five, Conagra and Utz sit at the **centralized** pole — single Customer Leadership Organization / sales force selling the full portfolio to all retailers, with brand marketing federated under segment or brand-portfolio leaders. Post sits at the **federated** pole — true holding-company model where each operating company has its own complete commercial stack and customers see multiple Post sales teams. B&G and TreeHouse occupy the unstable middle, oscillating between functional-commercial (CCO over brand presidents) and BU-presidential (BU presidents owning their own commercial functions) every few years.

**The "centralize Sales / federate Marketing" pattern is the dominant rollup playbook.** Conagra is the textbook case: De La Mater's Customer Leadership Organization is *the* single front door for retailers; segment presidents (O'Mara R&F, etc.) own brand teams and innovation. Utz follows the same model at smaller scale. The logic is straightforward: retailers want one Walmart team, one Kroger team — the cost of multiple sales calls outweighs any benefit of brand-dedicated reps. But brand stewardship and innovation cadence are brand-specific, so marketing federates. **The exception is Post, where the OpCos sell to genuinely different channels** (foodservice via Michael Foods vs retail cereal via PCB) — different channel mix justifies different sales orgs.

**RGM/Trade Marketing universally sits in Sales, not Finance.** All five place trade marketing and revenue growth management inside the customer-facing organization — Utz most explicitly ("Schreiber has responsibility for managing trade marketing/revenue management"), Conagra via job-posting evidence, B&G historically under Greenberg as CCO. None reports to the CFO. The reason: trade spend is a customer-negotiation lever, not a finance-controlled budget. RGM analytics may sit centrally and feed all segments, but the function lives in commercial.

**The Chief Commercial Officer role is integration scaffolding, not a permanent fixture.** B&G eliminated CCO and CCO-Customer titles in 2024 by promoting holders to BU presidents. TreeHouse eliminated CCO in 2025. Conagra never had a corporate CCO post-Pinnacle — split into Chief Customer Officer (sales) and COO (segments). Utz consolidated into a single Chief Customer Officer + EVP Sales hybrid. **The pattern: CCO is the right role during integration phases when you need a single neck for go-to-market consolidation. Once integration is complete, the work splits — into segment presidents (B&G), into a Chief Customer Officer narrowed to sales (Conagra/Utz), or it disappears entirely (TreeHouse).**

**Segment presidents own P&L, brand marketing, and innovation — not sales.** This is the consistent steady-state pattern. Conagra's O'Mara owns Refrigerated & Frozen P&L but doesn't have her own sales force. B&G's new BU presidents own brand portfolios. Post's OpCo CEOs are the exception — they own everything because the OpCos genuinely operate as separate businesses. **For GPG with five legacy entities consolidating into one commercial function, the relevant model is Conagra/B&G — segment presidents own P&L and brand-building; sales is unified.**

**What changes during integration vs. steady state.** Integration phase: temporary CCO role, often dual-hatted with a division presidency; emphasis on synergy capture, customer team consolidation, redundant role elimination. Steady state: CCO either narrows to Chief Customer Officer (sales-only) or splits into COO (segments) + CCO (sales) — Conagra's exact pattern. Marketing federates to segments early and stays there. RGM and shopper insights centralize during integration and stay centralized as shared services.
