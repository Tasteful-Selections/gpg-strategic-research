## 1. Category Performance: How Carrots Are Doing at Retail

### The Big Picture

The US carrot category is a **$2 billion annual market** (USDA 2025), making it one of the largest single-vegetable categories in produce. Fresh market accounts for roughly 80% of production volume and 97% of the category's dollar value — this is overwhelmingly a fresh retail story.

**Consumer demand is expanding.** Carrot purchase frequency hit 60.9% of US households in 2024, up from 50% the prior year (Fresh Trends). That 11-point jump in a single year is unusual for an established vegetable — it signals that carrots are gaining occasions, not just holding steady.

**Where the growth is coming from:**

| Segment | Trend | What's Driving It |
|---|---|---|
| **Baby carrots** | Stable, consistent demand | Snacking occasions, kids' lunches, convenience. ~$33B healthy snack market expanding at 5.8% CAGR. 42% of millennials prefer baby carrots as a healthy snack. |
| **Organic (all formats)** | Growing 5x faster than conventional | Ranked #6 in organic produce sales (~$450-550M). 17% of carrot buyers now shop organic exclusively, up from 14% prior year. |
| **Whole/bunch carrots** | Steady, cooking-driven | Home cooking and meal prep occasions. Juicing trend continues as a secondary driver. |
| **Value-added (chips, sticks, blends)** | Emerging | 39% of new carrot product intros in 2024 were convenience formats. Early but growing. |
| **Bulk/foodservice** | Seasonal variability | Restaurant and institutional demand tracks broader foodservice recovery. |

### Retail Channel Context

Produce departments continue to punch above their weight in total store performance:
- **61% of multi-store shoppers** say produce freshness determines which store they visit (FMI 2025)
- Produce accounts for **11% of total store sales** and is the only fresh department that grew its share in 2024
- **Fresh departments collectively = 42%** of total store revenue

The carrot set is a visible indicator of produce department quality — it's one of the categories shoppers notice.

## 2. Supply and Market Dynamics: What Happened in Q1

### Domestic Production

California remains the center of gravity for US carrot production at **71-74% of national volume**, with Washington (~23%) and Wisconsin (~5%) rounding out the commercial base. The three-state concentration means California growing conditions drive the national supply picture.

**Q1 2026 conditions:** California's State Water Project allocation came in at 10% — the lowest allocation in recent years and a constraint on all Kern County agriculture, including carrots. This created tighter-than-normal winter supply, particularly for whole and bulk segments where spot purchasing is more common. Baby carrot programs, which run on longer-term contracted supply, saw less disruption.

**Organic acreage** expanded approximately 36% in 2024 as growers responded to retail demand signals. The supply side is building to meet organic growth, though it takes 3 years for newly transitioned acreage to reach certified organic production — so the supply build has a lag.

### Import Dynamics

Imports provide supplemental volume during seasonal transitions. Over the Nov 2025 - Mar 2026 period, approximately **257 million lbs** of carrots were imported (USDA MARS), with:

| Source | Share | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Mexico | ~50% (trending to 64% by March) | Primary winter/spring supplement — peaks as Canadian storage depletes |
| Canada | ~46% (trending to 28% by March) | Post-harvest storage shipments — dominant in fall, fades by spring |
| Guatemala/Israel/Other | ~4% | Niche volumes, not a significant factor |

The seasonal crossover from Canadian to Mexican supply happened in January, roughly on schedule. Import volumes ran at 14-16M lbs/week through Q1 — consistent with recent years.

**Key entry points:** 70% of Mexican volume comes through Pharr, TX. Canadian volume enters primarily through Lake Champlain, NY and Buffalo, NY. These are relevant for logistics planning and regional supply timing.

### What This Means for Q2-Q3

- California production moves into peak season through summer, but water constraints will shape both volume and cost (see Growing Conditions below)
- Import volumes will moderate as domestic availability increases
- Organic supply remains the tightest segment — demand continues to outrun the acreage build-out
- Columbia Basin (WA) summer production is an important supply bridge — monitor PNW drought conditions

## Growing Region Conditions: Weather and Water Outlook

*The single biggest short-term variable in fresh carrot supply is weather. This section tracks conditions across the major growing regions.*

### California — Kern County / San Joaquin Valley (71-74% of US production)

**Water supply is the headline.** California's April 1 snowpack survey measured just **18% of average statewide** — and only **6% in the Northern Sierra**, where the major reservoirs sit. A record-hot March (Bakersfield saw mid-90s, roughly 20 degrees above normal) melted the pack weeks ahead of schedule.

- **State Water Project** allocation was 30% as of late January. Given the snowpack collapse, the next revision is likely downward.
- **Central Valley Project** south-of-delta ag contractors are at **20% of contract** — surface water is severely constrained. Growers are relying more on groundwater pumping, which increases costs.
- The record March heat also accelerated crop development timelines and increased water demand at exactly the wrong time.

**Outlook:** Q2-Q3 is the tightest window — between the end of desert-region harvest (Imperial/Coachella wraps by April) and before fall Pacific Northwest product comes in. Water costs will be elevated. Quality monitoring on heat-stressed product is warranted.

### Washington — Columbia Basin (~23% of US production)

**Snow drought but infrastructure buffer.** Washington snowpack is at **51% of normal** (Oregon at 30%, its second-lowest in 46 years). However, the Columbia Basin Project draws from Grand Coulee/Banks Lake storage, providing more resilience than snowmelt-dependent systems.

- South Columbia Basin Irrigation District began 2026 deliveries on March 17 — on schedule
- NOAA's spring outlook calls for drought expansion through the PNW and Central Rockies by mid-summer
- Yakima Basin junior water right holders at just 44% of normal — a proxy for broader PNW tightness

**Outlook:** Washington summer production should proceed but watch for possible delivery curtailments if conditions don't improve by June/July. Bureau of Reclamation storage updates will be the key signal.

### Wisconsin — Central Wisconsin (~5% of US production)

**Wet spring, not dry.** Unlike the West, Wisconsin's concern is excess moisture. AccuWeather projects warmer weather won't settle in until May, with 33-40% chance of above-normal precipitation through spring.

- Risk: soggy fields could delay planting (late April-May window)
- If planting delays, harvest timing shifts later into fall
- No drought or water concerns — the opposite of the western story

**Outlook:** No supply threat — possible timing shift only. Monitor field conditions through late April.

### Mexico (Winter/Spring Import Source)

**Limited visibility.** Current-season production data from Mexican growing regions (Guanajuato, Puebla, Baja California) is not publicly available. No negative signals from the Q1 import volume data — Mexican shipments ran at expected levels through Pharr, TX.

**Outlook:** If California production is constrained in Q3-Q4, Mexican supply becomes more important for the US market. Worth monitoring through trade contacts and broker intelligence.

### ENSO Outlook — The Macro Signal

The La Nina phase has ended. NOAA projects **El Nino emergence by June-August** (62% probability), with some forecasters flagging a potentially strong event by late summer.

- **For Q2-Q3 2026:** ENSO-neutral conditions. No major pattern shift expected this growing season.
- **For winter 2026-27:** El Nino winters in California typically bring above-average rainfall — which could meaningfully improve the water picture for 2027, but won't help the current season.

### Net Weather Assessment

| Region | Status | Key Risk | Watch For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **California (Kern/SJV)** | Stressed | Water allocation, heat | SWP/CVP allocation updates, summer temps |
| **Washington (Columbia Basin)** | Cautious | Regional snow drought | June/July Reclamation storage levels |
| **Wisconsin** | Normal | Wet spring planting delays | Field access through late April |
| **Mexico** | Limited visibility | Data gap | Q3-Q4 import volumes if CA tightens |
| **ENSO** | Transitioning | El Nino emerging | Summer pattern shift, 2027 winter relief |

## 3. The Full Mix: Segment-by-Segment View

### Baby Carrots

The anchor of the retail carrot category. Baby carrots are the most **demand-stable** format because they run on planned retail programs rather than spot purchasing. Q1 showed:

- Consistent weekly volume with minimal supply disruption
- Snacking occasions continue to expand — not just kids' lunches but workplace snacking, paired snacking (carrots + hummus), and on-the-go formats
- Conventional baby carrots remain the volume driver; organic baby is the fastest-growing sub-segment

**Category opportunity:** Cross-merchandising baby carrots with hummus and dips remains one of the highest-ROI produce merchandising plays. Retailers reporting incremental volume lifts from secondary displays near the deli or snack sections.

### Organic Carrots

The **highest-growth segment** in the carrot category and one of the top-performing organic produce items nationally.

- Organic carrot sales grew **5.1%** in 2024 (OPN), outpacing the broader carrot category
- **31% of US households** now buy organic vegetables (OTA); organic carrot penetration is rising within that base
- Organic produce overall grew **5x faster** than conventional in 2025 (+5.9% vs. +1.3%)
- Gen Z and millennial shoppers are driving the shift — 1 in 3 millennials say the majority of their produce purchases are organic (Fresh Trends 2025)

**The demographic tailwind:** The K-shaped economy is concentrating grocery spending growth at the premium end. Households earning $150K+ increased their share of US food spending from 19% to 24% between 2022 and 2025. Fresh-format grocers catering to these shoppers posted the strongest traffic gains of any grocery segment in 2025 (Placer.ai). Organic carrots sit squarely in this growth lane.

**Assortment consideration:** Many retailers carry organic baby carrots but are underweight on organic whole carrots (2lb/5lb bags) for the cooking and meal prep occasion. This is a whitespace worth evaluating — the organic whole carrot shopper is often a different buyer than the organic baby carrot shopper.

### Whole and Bunch Carrots

The **cooking and meal prep** segment. Whole carrots serve a fundamentally different occasion than baby — they're a recipe ingredient, not a snack. Demand tracks home cooking trends.

- Q1 saw tighter-than-normal supply due to California water conditions
- Whole/bunch carrots are more exposed to spot market dynamics than contracted baby programs
- Rainbow and specialty varieties (purple, yellow, multi-color) continue to gain specialty/premium shelf space — these are visual merchandising assets as much as sales drivers

### Bulk and Foodservice

Bulk carrots (25lb and 50lb sacks) serve foodservice and institutional channels. This segment:

- Shows the most seasonal price variability across the carrot category
- Tracks broader foodservice recovery trends
- Mexico-sourced bulk plays a larger role in this segment than in retail-packaged formats

## 4. Consumer Spotlight: The Organic Carrot Buyer

*Each issue features a consumer insight relevant to carrot category planning.*

### This Quarter: Why Organic Loyalty Compounds

The organic carrot buyer isn't just a premium shopper — they're a **retaining** shopper. Industry data shows that younger consumers who enter the organic category tend to stay:

- **17% of carrot buyers** now shop organic exclusively, up from 14% prior year (The Packer/Fresh Trends)
- **Over two-thirds of younger consumers** seek the organic label in almost every food purchase (OTA)
- **77% of Millennial and Gen Z shoppers** describe organic claims as at least somewhat important vs. 55% of Gen X and Boomers

This matters for category planning because organic carrot shelf space invested today isn't serving a trend — it's building a long-term customer base. As millennials and Gen Z age into peak grocery spending years (household formation, kids, higher income), their organic preference comes with them. The organic carrot buyer acquired at 28 is still an organic carrot buyer at 38 and 48.

**Actionable implication:** Evaluate organic carrot assortment depth against the demographic profile of your trade area. Stores in markets with younger, higher-income shopper bases should be leading on organic — not matching the chain average.

## 5. Nutritional Note: The Vitamin A Advantage

Carrots have a nutritional claim no other major vegetable can match.

| Nutrient (per 100g, raw) | Carrots | Cabbage | Eggplant |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Vitamin A** | **835 mcg RAE (100%+ DV)** | 5 mcg | 1 mcg |
| Potassium | 320 mg | 170 mg | 229 mg |
| Fiber | 2.8g | 2.5g | 3.0g |

One serving of carrots delivers over 100% of the daily recommended Vitamin A intake. For in-store signage, this is a clean and differentiated health callout — particularly effective near the baby carrot display where snacking shoppers are making impulse decisions.

*Source: USDA FoodData Central*

## 6. What to Watch Next Quarter

- **California water updates** — SWP and CVP allocation revisions expected. If allocations hold at current levels or decline, Q3 supply tightness in whole/bulk segments is likely. Baby carrot programs on contracted supply are more insulated.
- **Columbia Basin irrigation** — June/July Bureau of Reclamation storage levels will signal whether WA summer production faces curtailments
- **El Nino development** — NOAA's June update will indicate timing and strength. A strong El Nino could reshape summer/fall weather but would be most impactful for the 2026-27 winter outlook.
- **Organic supply vs. demand gap** — new certified acreage coming online but demand growth may still outpace supply through 2026
- **Back-to-school baby carrot demand** (Aug/Sep) — the annual peak occasion for baby carrot programs; plan promotional timing accordingly
- **Healthy snacking competition** — watch for new produce snack formats (snap peas, mini peppers) competing for the same shelf space and occasion baby carrots own
